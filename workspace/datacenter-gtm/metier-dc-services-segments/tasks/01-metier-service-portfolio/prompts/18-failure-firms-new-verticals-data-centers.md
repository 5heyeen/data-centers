# Prompt 18: failure-firms-new-verticals-data-centers

> **Prompt:** When firms enter new verticals (like data centers), what services do they mistakenly lead with vs. what actually gets traction? Why?

---

## New Vertical Entry: What Firms Lead With vs. What Gets Traction

**What firms mistakenly lead with:**
Abstract, relationship-stage-inappropriate services — organisational assessments, maturity models, transformation roadmaps, portfolio strategy. These require significant trust and a long sales cycle. They also implicitly ask the buyer to acknowledge inadequacy, which creates friction early. Firms default to their core competency framing rather than the buyer's problem framing.

**What actually gets traction:**
Almost always a bounded, high-stakes problem with a near-term deadline. In data centers specifically:
- Construction schedule recovery
- Procurement support for long-lead equipment (generators, UPS, switchgear — 40–80 week lead times)
- Commissioning planning
- Owner's representation during a capital raise or regulatory approval

These are concrete, time-boxed, and the cost of failure is visible. The buyer does not need to trust the firm's overall capabilities — just their ability to solve this specific problem now.

**The underlying dynamic:**
New-vertical entry requires credibility before scope. Buyers in specialised sectors are skeptical of generalists. The entry wedge is almost always a specific deliverable — a schedule, a risk register, a procurement strategy — not a framework or engagement model. Once a firm delivers on something specific and technical, the door opens to broader advisory work.

**Pitching to the wrong buyer:**
Firms default to the C-suite because that's where strategy conversations live. But in DC development, the project director, construction manager, or COO often controls the relevant budget and has the immediate pain. Entry through an operational problem — not a strategic one — is the more reliable path.

**Implication for Metier:**
Lead with owner's engineer and procurement support for specific projects — not with strategy advisory or organisational design. Win the first engagement on delivery; earn the right to the strategic conversation.
