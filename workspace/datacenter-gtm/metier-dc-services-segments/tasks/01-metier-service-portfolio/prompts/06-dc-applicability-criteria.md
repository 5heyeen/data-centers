> **Prompt:** Criteria for DC applicability of PM services

## What Determines Whether a PM Consultancy Service Applies to the Data Center Sector

Three factors determine genuine applicability. Each corresponds to a structural feature of DC projects that creates real failure risk when a PM firm lacks it.

### 1. DC Technical Complexity — MEP, Power, Cooling

In a data center, the MEP systems are the product. The power chain — HV intake, transformers, MV distribution, UPS, PDUs — and the cooling plant are engineered with N+1 or 2N redundancy and are tightly interdependent. A cooling design cannot be finalised without a frozen power envelope; the mechanical room slab cannot be poured until cooling layout is set. A PM firm that treats MEP as the contractor's problem loses schedule control at the points where it matters most. Applicability requires that the PM team understands which design decisions create lock-in, and when those decisions must land relative to procurement and construction milestones.

### 2. Client Types — PE-Backed Developers, Colo Operators, Hyperscalers

These three buyer types have materially different PM needs. Hyperscalers run internal PMOs and expect suppliers to integrate into their systems, not impose new ones. Colo operators are revenue-sensitive — a delayed data hall is a delayed lease — and need tight phased delivery management. PE-backed developers operate under milestone-gated capital tranches and investor reporting obligations; they need the PM function to translate physical progress into financial forecasts (IRR impact of schedule variance, contingency drawdown). A consultancy must identify which archetype its service model fits. Claiming all three without differentiation is a weak signal.

### 3. Procurement Norms — EPC/EPCM and Long-Lead Equipment

Critical-path items on most DC builds are not civil or structural — they are equipment: transformers, switchgear, generators, and UPS systems with 40–80 week manufacturing lead times. Procurement must begin at or before FEED, not after detailed design. The contract model governs who holds that risk: under EPC, the contractor owns procurement; under EPCM, the owner takes it in exchange for cost transparency. A PM firm that cannot advise on this choice, or that defaults to a single model regardless of context, is applying a generic framework to a sector-specific problem.
