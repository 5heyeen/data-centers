## Research Summary

### 1. Concept Research Programme — Front-End Quality as Primary Cause

The Concept research programme at NTNU is Norway's leading academic programme on major public investment project quality. Its findings on cost overruns are directly relevant.

**Key finding across multiple Concept reports:** "Deficiencies in the early phase of projects, before the decision to initiate the project was made, was the overarching main cause of large cost overruns in project implementation." — [Concept/NTNU research, summarised in WebSearch snippet referencing multiple Concept reports, 2026-04-25]

**Concept rapport nr. 25 — Hospital Planning Regime:** "Regime for planlegging og beslutning i sykehusprosjekter" (Regime for planning and decision-making in hospital projects) — [NTNU Concept: https://www.yumpu.com/no/document/view/30026769/regime-for-planlegging-og-beslutning-i-sykehus-concept-ntnu] — This report specifically addresses how Norwegian hospital projects are planned and decided upon, identifying structural weaknesses in the decision-making framework.

**Concept rapport nr. 51:** "Stanse svake prosjektforslag oftere og tidligere?" (Stop weak project proposals more often and earlier?) — [NTNU: https://www.ntnu.edu/documents/1261865083/0/Webversjon_Concept-rapport+nr.+68+norsk.pdf — 403 on fetch] — addresses whether the KS/quality assurance scheme succeeds in stopping weak project proposals.

**Concept research on KS2 cost control:** "Kostnadskontroll i statlige prosjekter med og uten ekstern kvalitetssikring" — [Concept/NTNU Rapport 1277: https://www.ntnu.no/documents/1261860271/1262021752/Rapport+1277_sluttrapport_endelig.pdf — 403 on fetch] — examines cost performance in projects with and without external quality assurance.

### 2. "Budsjettsprekk er normalen" — Stat & Styring

The policy journal Stat & Styring published an article titled "Budsjettsprekk er normalen" (Budget overrun is the norm) — [https://www.statogstyring.no/budsjettsprekk-er-normalen/ — 403 on fetch]. The title itself is a finding: in Norwegian public sector projects, cost overrun is the expected outcome, not the exception.

### 3. Systemic Causes — Synthesis from Available Evidence

Based on the research across Prompts 1–7, the following systemic causes emerge for Norwegian hospital megaprojects:

**a) Systematic underestimation / optimism bias in early-phase cost estimates**

The trajectory across all current Norwegian hospital megaprojects shows consistent cost escalation from initial estimates to outturn:
- Nye Rikshospitalet/Nye Aker: P50 NOK 36,045M (2021) → 44,700M (2024) → projected 50B+ at completion
- SUS2023: ~11.2B approved → ~12.5B at practical completion (+11%)

This is consistent with the "planning fallacy" (Kahneman/Lovallo) and systematic optimism bias documented in megaproject research globally (Flyvbjerg et al.).

**b) Demand/capacity mismatch — planned for too few patients**

The Ahus case (designed for 340,000 catchment, opened serving 470,000+) illustrates a structural problem in Norwegian hospital project planning: population projections are systematically underestimated, resulting in undersized facilities that require expensive post-opening adaptation.

**c) Political compression of budgets before parliamentary approval**

The Ahus case documents how political pressure to keep budget estimates low (Minister Høybråten's 2003 budget compression, referenced in search snippets) results in approved budgets that are not feasible, forcing scope reduction or cost overruns.

**d) Post-2021 construction market inflation — structural exposure**

SUS2023 explicitly: "The approved cost framework included an annual price adjustment of 3 percent, but actual price increases from 2021 onward have been significantly higher." — [WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]

Traditional contracts that assumed 3% annual price escalation created structural exposure when actual construction price inflation ran at 10-20% in 2021-2023. An open-book/target cost model would have given earlier visibility.

**e) Contractor conflicts in complex coordination environments**

NRK's reporting on SUS2023 ("Varslet katastrofe" — predicted catastrophe) and the Riksrevisjonen's systemic findings on health trust estate management both point to coordination failures between multiple contractors as a recurring problem. Split contracts (delte entrepriser) create interface risk that traditional contract models do not adequately manage.

**f) Norwegian hospital debt financing model — perverse incentives**

NRK Rogaland reported: "Gjeldskrise for norske sykehus: Betaler milliarder i renter til staten" — Norwegian health trusts are financed through state loans (not grants), creating a debt service burden that constrains operating budgets and creates pressure to underfund capital projects. This systemic financing structure means cost overruns have double consequences: increased capital cost AND increased debt service from operating budgets.

### 4. Riksrevisjonen Systemic Findings

The Riksrevisjonen has published sector-wide audits of health trust property management (eiendomsforvaltning) and investments in buildings and medical equipment, finding:
- Poor alignment between maintenance plans and upgrade needs
- Weak decision-making foundations for new hospital builds
- Financing pressure forcing scope cuts during project execution

— [Riksrevisjonen, referenced via Stortinget.no: https://www.stortinget.no/no/Saker-og-publikasjoner/Saker/Sak/?p=51172]

## Inferred Findings

**Inferred:** The combination of political budget compression, optimism bias in demand forecasting, split-contract interface risk, and the Norwegian debt-financing model creates a structural system in which Norwegian hospital megaproject cost overruns are almost inevitable under traditional procurement — regardless of project management quality.
**Based on:** "Deficiencies in the early phase of projects...was the overarching main cause of large cost overruns" — [Concept/NTNU, WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]; "actual price increases from 2021 onward have been significantly higher [than the 3% assumed]" — [Helse Stavanger / WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]
**Reasoning:** Front-end deficiencies (optimism bias in cost and demand) combine with traditional contract structures that defer risk to the market. When market conditions change (post-2021 inflation), the combination produces large overruns. A collaborative contracting model with open-book cost data and shared risk could have provided earlier warning signals and shared cost exposure, potentially enabling timely scope decisions rather than late-stage overrun absorption.
