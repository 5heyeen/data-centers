## Section 1 — Model Typology: From IPD-Lite to Full Multi-Party Alliance

The international landscape of collaborative contracting includes a spectrum of models. The key distinction is between "true" multi-party arrangements (shared risk pool, multi-party contract) and "IPD-lite" arrangements (collaborative philosophy without structural risk sharing).

### 1a. AIA IPD Family — Three Levels (US Framework)

"The AIA provides agreements for three levels of integrated project delivery:
1. **Transitional Forms** — modelled after existing construction manager agreements; first step into IPD without shared risk
2. **Multi-Party Agreement (IFOA)** — single agreement between owner, design professional, and constructor; shared risk/gain pool; open-book
3. **Single Purpose Entity (SPE)** — creates a limited liability company for the purpose of planning, designing, and constructing the project" — [WebSearch snippet referencing AIA Contract Documents FAQ: https://help.aiacontracts.com/hc/en-us/articles/1500010383781-FAQs-Integrated-Project-Delivery-IPD-Family-of-Documents, 2026-04-25]

"IPD as a Philosophy (IPD 'Lite' or 'IPD-ish') / Non Multi-party IPD" differs from "IPD as a Delivery Method ('True' IPD / Multi-party IPD)." — [WebSearch snippet referencing AGC IPD for Public and Private Owners: https://www.agc.org/sites/default/files/Files/Programs%20&%20Industry%20Relations/IPD%20for%20Public%20and%20Private%20Owners.pdf, 2026-04-25]

**Norwegian parallel:** Norwegian "samspill" in its most common form (totalentreprise med samhandling) corresponds approximately to AIA Level 1 (Transitional) — collaborative ECI phase, single contractor, no multi-party risk sharing. Norwegian "IPL" (Integrert Prosjektleveranse) corresponds to AIA Level 2 (Multi-Party Agreement/IFOA).


### 1b. ConsensusDocs 300 — Multi-Party IPD Agreement

ConsensusDocs 300 is an Integrated Form of Agreement (IFOA) signed by the Owner, Design Professional, and Constructor. It is used as an alternative to AIA IFOA, with similar multi-party risk-sharing structure. The ConsensusDocs 411 document covers Progressive Design-Build.

### 1c. Australian Project Alliance (APA Model)

The Australian project alliance is the most extensively documented large-scale multi-party collaborative model in infrastructure globally. Key structural features:
- Single multi-party contract between owner and all Non-Owner Participants (NOPs) — designer, contractor, key subcontractors
- Open-book cost reimbursement (owner pays defined direct costs)
- Risk/reward regime: pain/gain sharing against a Target Outturn Cost (TOC) with cap structure
- Integrated leadership through an Alliance Leadership Team (ALT) with unanimous-consent decision making
- No fault, no blame provisions (parties waive claims against each other)
- Widely used in Victoria, Queensland, Western Australia for road, rail, and water infrastructure

"The total value of alliance projects in the road, rail and water sectors in New South Wales, Victoria, Queensland and Western Australia, over the period 2004 to 2009 was $32 billion." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Performance of Project Alliancing in Australasia — UTS ePress/ResearchGate: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/275221924_Performance_of_Project_Alliancing_in_Australasia_a_Digest_of_Infrastructure_Development_from_2008_to_2013, 2026-04-25]

"94.5% of project owners and 97.3% of non-owner participants believed that their alliance met or exceeded the requirements [in a study of alliance projects valued over AU$100M from 2004 to 2009]." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Performance of Project Alliancing in Australasia, UTS ePress, 2026-04-25]

**Scale:** Australian alliances have included individual projects of AU$500M–2B+ (rail and water). The $32B portfolio figure confirms systematic use at very large aggregate scale, though individual project scale is typically AU$200M–1B+, below the 20B NOK target.


### 1d. NEC4 Alliance Contract (UK)

The NEC4 Alliance Contract (ALC) was launched in June 2018 and creates a "true" alliance arrangement in which the client and all key supply chain members (called "Partners") are engaged under a single contract.

**Key structural features:**
- Single contract for all partners (client + design firms + contractors + key subcontractors)
- Payment on basis of defined cost (open-book / cost-reimbursable)
- Pain/gain share: measured against total alliance cost vs. budget (total alliance cost = client's costs + defined cost + fee of partners)
- Performance table: partners incentivised through performance measurement beyond just cost
- Designed for major projects or programmes of work "where longer-term collaborative ways of working are to be created"

"The NEC4 Alliance Contract includes an assessment of pain or gain share if the alliance's costs...is above or below the budget." — [WebSearch snippet referencing NEC Contract official: https://www.neccontract.com/news/introducing-the-new-nec4-alliance-contract, 2026-04-25]

"The most commonly used standard form alliance contracts in the UK include the NEC4 Alliance Contract, along with TAC-1, FAC-1, PPC2000, and JCT Constructing Excellence Contract 2016." — [WebSearch snippet referencing DLA Piper Alliance Contracting analysis: https://www.dlapiper.com/en/insights/publications/2021/03/alliance-contracting-in-the-uk-infrastructure-industry, 2026-04-25]

**Scale evidence:** No confirmed large hospital case under NEC4 ALC was retrieved. The NEC4 ALC was launched in 2018 and is relatively new; large-scale examples are primarily in UK rail and water (Thames Tideway Tunnel, HS2 early works used NEC contracts though not full alliance). Hospital-specific NEC4 ALC case studies were not found in search results.

### 1e. Progressive Design-Build (PDB) / Two-Stage Open Book

Progressive Design-Build is a two-stage approach: Phase 1 (collaborative open-book design development with contractor, producing a GMP) → Phase 2 (fixed-price execution at the agreed GMP/target cost).

"Progressive design-build is highly collaborative, and the owner is generally highly involved in estimating and budgeting discussions. The Contract Price for Phase 2 shall be developed during Phase 1 on an 'open-book' basis. After the project design is 50 to 75% complete, the design-builder issues a Guaranteed Maximum Price (GMP)." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Procore/Kiwi Newton guides, 2026-04-25]

"Infrastructure Ontario's 'Progressive' procurement strategy fosters collaboration between the owner and its contracting partner...a 12-to-18-month development agreement phase with contractor involvement...at the end fix the price for those works and enter into a well-understood contract." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Infrastructure Ontario model selection: https://www.infrastructureontario.ca/en/what-we-do/major-projects/model-selection/, 2026-04-25]

## Section 2 — Scale Evidence: What Has Been Demonstrated

### 2a. Multi-Party IPD (AIA IFOA / IFOA-equivalent) — Hospital Sector

**Largest single project documented:**
- Sutter Health CPMC Van Ness Campus: $2.1B USD (≈ 22–24B NOK at 2024 exchange rates), $150M under budget — see Task 03 for full citation.

**Portfolio scale:**
- Sutter Health: $1.5B across 15 projects (2007–2015), all on time and on budget — see Task 03.
- HDR (architecture/engineering firm): 32 IPD projects, average 12.8% cost savings over 7 years. — [WebSearch snippet referencing HDR: https://www.hdrinc.com/services/project-delivery/benefits-integrated-project-delivery, 2026-04-25]

**Claimed performance metric (treat with caution):**
"IPD has achieved a success rate greater than 98% against established quantitative project performance measures." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Healthcare Facilities Today / generic IPD advocacy sources, 2026-04-25; methodology not confirmed — this claim circulates widely in IPD marketing materials without clear primary source]

"Multi-party contracts through the Integrated Form of Agreement promote shared risk and joint accountability, resulting in improved trust and cooperation among parties, as evidenced by a 20% cost reduction in projects." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Umbrex/generic IPD summary, 2026-04-25; primary source not confirmed]

**Critical scale gap note:** The Sutter CPMC Van Ness at $2.1B is the only documented hospital IPD project at the 20B NOK target scale. All other documented hospital IPD cases are below $500M USD. There is a sparse documented evidence base at the 10B+ NOK hospital scale for multi-party IPD.

### 2b. Australian Project Alliance — Infrastructure Scale

- $32B total portfolio 2004–2009 across road, rail, and water in four Australian states
- Individual projects: AU$200M to several billion (specific project values in rail: e.g., $705M New Bunbury-Perth High delivered under budget)
- No confirmed hospital alliance case in Australian search results
- Victoria continues to use alliancing in rail: "there are still billions of AU$ in public infrastructure investments in the rail sector, particularly in Victoria, where Alliancing is still seen as the most appropriate method for delivery" — [WebSearch snippet referencing Roads & Infrastructure Magazine / UTS ePress, 2026-04-25]


### 2c. Progressive Design-Build — Hospital Sector (Canada)

Infrastructure Ontario has applied Progressive Design-Build-Finance-Maintain (Progressive DBFM) to major hospital projects:

**Ottawa Hospital Civic Campus Redevelopment:**
- Scale: "more than two million square feet" — the largest healthcare infrastructure project in Ottawa's history
- Model: Progressive Design-Build-Finance-Maintain (DBFM)
- Status: Under procurement/delivery as of 2024–2025
- Outcome: Not yet confirmed (under construction/delivery)

"Infrastructure Ontario and Weeneebayko Area Health Authority Launch a C$1.8 billion Progressive Design-Build Project" — [Fasken: https://www.fasken.com/en/experience/2024/10/infrastructure-ontario-and-weeneebayko-area-health-authority-progressive-design-build-project, WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]

"Infrastructure Ontario's first progressive design-build finance project and the first progressive project in the social infrastructure space." — [Canada Construction Connect / WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]

**Claimed value-for-money outcome (Infrastructure Ontario claim):**
"Based on precedent project experience, a DBFM procurement model typically has delivered a value for money savings in the range of 15% to 22% compared to undertaking the same projects using traditional delivery models." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Infrastructure Ontario procurement model selection page: https://www.infrastructureontario.ca/en/what-we-do/major-projects/model-selection/, 2026-04-25]

**Note on Progressive DBFM vs. Multi-Party IPD:** The Infrastructure Ontario Progressive DBFM model retains risk transfer to the private sector in Phase 2 (unlike true multi-party IPD which maintains shared risk throughout). It is a hybrid: collaborative design development in Phase 1 (IPD-like), followed by a fixed-price financed delivery in Phase 2 (PPP-like). This is closer to Norwegian "totalentreprise med samhandling" (ECI + implementation contract) than to full multi-party IPL.

## Section 3 — Structural Conditions Identified as Enabling Large-Scale IPD

Based on the search results across Tasks 03 and 04, the following structural conditions appear in the evidence as enabling factors for multi-party IPD/alliance at large scale:

**a) Owner organisational maturity and sustained commitment**
"IPD has achieved a success rate greater than 98%..." claim is driven largely by Sutter Health, which spent 10+ years developing its IPD capability and IFOA contract template before applying it to $2.1B projects. Owner IPD capability is not a one-project learning.

**b) Target Value Design (TVD) discipline**
"Target Value Design used in IPD confirms that specific systems meet a budget and commit to them during design to ensure coordination." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Healthcare Facilities Today, 2026-04-25]
TVD is the mechanism that converts the target cost into a binding design constraint — without disciplined TVD, the shared risk pool does not produce cost savings.


**c) Co-location / Big Room**
"240 design professionals working together in the same 'big room' environment" — documented for Sutter CPMC Van Ness — [Task 03; SmithGroup/WebSearch snippet]. Co-location at megaproject scale requires purpose-built BIM/collaboration infrastructure. At 20B NOK scale, "big room" becomes a cluster structure (sub-teams for building systems, equipment packages, etc.).

**d) Liability waivers between IPD parties**
"The IPD agreement used in the Tønsberg project states that the parties waive and release claims and liability between each other, with the IPD team holding joint responsibility for the project during design and execution until the hand-over." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Emerald Insight / Benefits and challenges to applying IPD, 2026-04-25]
Liability waivers between parties are structurally necessary for multi-party IPD — without them, each party retains individual risk and the shared risk pool cannot function. In Norwegian public contracts, the permissibility of mutual liability waivers under public procurement law requires legal analysis (Task 05).

**e) Open-book cost accounting throughout**
All documented successful IPD and alliance models rely on open-book cost accounting (owner sees actual costs, not just invoices). This changes the information structure: the owner can intervene on scope decisions before they become cost facts. In traditional contracts, the owner learns about cost overruns after they occur.

**f) Scale management via clustering / packages**
At megaproject scale (>$500M / >5B NOK), no single IPD team can encompass the entire project complexity. The documented approach is:
- Sutter Health: Multiple separate IPD project agreements for different buildings/facilities (15 projects over 8 years)
- Australian alliances: Each alliance project is a separate multi-party contract (not one alliance for a $32B programme)
- There is no documented single IPD/alliance contract at 20B NOK hospital scale covering all scopes in one agreement

## Section 5 — Evidence on Value-for-Money at Scale

**Debate on Australian alliance "value for money":**
"Although alliance contracting has been widely used in more than 200 complex infrastructure projects in Australia, there has been an ongoing debate on the question of 'value for money' and whether or not alliance contracts deliver better project outcomes compared to projects undertaken in a more price competitive environment." — [WebSearch snippet referencing Roads & Infrastructure Magazine / UTS ePress, 2026-04-25]

This debate matters for megaproject-scale application: at very large scale, the "no competition" element of alliance contracts (where the owner pays defined cost + fee, with no competitive bidding for the execution work) creates questions about whether the risk/gain structure adequately incentivises cost efficiency compared to competitive tendering.

**The Victorian response:** Victoria moved away from pure project alliances in the 2010s due to value-for-money concerns, introducing competitive elements and hybrid models (Managing Contractor, Early Contractor Involvement hybrids) before partially returning to alliancing for complex rail projects where competitive tendering had failed.

**The Norwegian counter-argument:** In Norwegian hospital megaprojects, the "competitive tendering" baseline has produced confirmed 11–24% cost overruns (SUS2023, OUS/Nye Rikshospitalet). The value-for-money comparison must be made against the Norwegian traditional model's actual outturn, not a theoretical competitive ideal.

## Inferred Findings

**Inferred:** Multi-party IPD (AIA IFOA equivalent) has been demonstrated at hospital megaproject scale ($2.1B USD / ~22B NOK) with a positive budget outcome, but the evidence base at this scale is thin — essentially one project (Sutter CPMC Van Ness) with confirmed budget performance, supported by a portfolio of smaller Sutter projects.
**Based on:** "$150 million under the original project budget" on $2.1B Van Ness project — [SmithGroup/LCI Congress, WebSearch snippet, Task 03]; "94.5% of project owners believed their alliance met or exceeded requirements" — [UTS ePress Australasia study, WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]
**Reasoning:** The Sutter CPMC Van Ness result is a single data point at megaproject scale. The broader Sutter portfolio (15 projects, $1.5B total) and HDR's 32 projects provide a pattern suggesting that IPD can consistently deliver hospitals on or below budget, but this evidence base is overwhelmingly US-specific and concentrated in one health system (Sutter). For Norwegian public hospital procurement, this evidence supports feasibility but does not prove that comparable results would be achieved.


**Inferred:** No single multi-party IPD or alliance contract has been documented at full 20B NOK equivalent hospital scale in a single contract scope. The evidence suggests that at this scale, the workable model is a programme of separate but aligned IPD/alliance contracts (by building, by package, or by phase) rather than a single monolithic multi-party agreement.
**Based on:** Sutter Health deployed 15 separate IPD projects rather than one master agreement; Australian alliance used separate contracts per project within programmes — [Berkeley P2SL/UTS ePress, WebSearch snippets, 2026-04-25]; "Too many elements were attempted implemented at once" at the Tønsberg project (44,500 m²) — [Emerald Insight/WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]
**Reasoning:** The structural complexity of a hospital project at 20B NOK (>100,000 m², multiple building clusters, distinct MEP/IT/equipment packages) makes a single all-encompassing multi-party IPD agreement operationally unmanageable. The Tønsberg Project found that involving too many participants created coordination overload at 44,500 m². At seven times that scale, a programme structure — multiple aligned IPD/samspill packages with a coordinating owner organisation — appears to be the only workable model variant.

**Inferred:** The "Progressive Design-Build" / "totalentreprise med samhandling" family of models (two-stage: collaborative development → fixed price execution) is the closest international analog to current Norwegian samspill practice, but it does not achieve genuine multi-party risk sharing. It is a procurement efficiency tool, not a risk-alignment tool.
**Based on:** "At the end [of Phase 1], fix the price for those works and enter into a well-understood contract" — Infrastructure Ontario model — [WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]; "Totalentreprise med samhandling...etter samhandlingsfasen var planen å inngå gjennomføringskontrakt" — [Sykehusbygg HF snippet, Task 01 research]
**Reasoning:** Both Progressive DBFM and Norwegian totalentreprise med samhandling convert collaborative early-phase work into a fixed-price or target-cost execution contract. This reduces procurement risk and improves design quality, but does not maintain shared risk between owner and contractor through the execution phase. When construction inflation or scope complexity exceeds the GMP/gjennomføringskontrakt assumption, cost overruns revert to the owner (or produce contractor disputes). To achieve genuine shared risk through execution, the model must maintain open-book cost accounting and pain/gain sharing through handover — which is the structural difference between samspill/PDB and true IPD/alliance.

## Unverified Observations

- The HDR "12.8% cost savings across 32 IPD projects" and the "98% success rate" claims circulate widely in IPD promotional materials. No primary academic source documenting the methodology behind these claims was retrieved. They should be treated as indicative rather than independently verified.
- The Lean Construction Institute (LCI) and AIA maintain repositories of IPD case studies, but these were not fetched (likely 403 or paywall). The AIA IPD Case Studies document (2012 and later editions) is the primary reference source.
- The Ottawa Hospital Civic Campus (>2M sq.ft., Progressive DBFM) has not been completed; no budget outcome data is available. It will be the most important North American reference case when it completes.
- The NEC4 Alliance Contract has limited published outcome data — it was launched in 2018 and major projects using it are still under delivery. The Thames Estuary Asset Management Programme (TEAM) and some UK water sector programmes use NEC4 alliance variants, but specific project financial outcomes were not retrieved.
- No academic study directly comparing IPD vs. traditional contracting outcomes at hospital megaproject scale (>$1B USD) was found. The Tønsberg studies compare IPD against traditional at small scale; the Sutter Health evidence is internal/practitioner-reported, not independently audited.
- The value-for-money debate in Australian alliance contracting (moved away from pure alliancing in 2010s, then partial return) is extensively documented by the Victorian Department of Treasury and Finance and Infrastructure Partnerships Australia, but specific reports were not fetched.
