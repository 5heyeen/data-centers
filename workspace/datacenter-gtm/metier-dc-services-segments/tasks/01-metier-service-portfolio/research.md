## Section: Principles

### What signals indicate genuine transferability to a new vertical like data centers?
Genuine transferability rests on three elements: transferable process, transferable risk literacy, and personnel depth. Five indicators: (1) repeated execution in structurally analogous projects (pharma cleanrooms, semiconductor fabs, grid infrastructure); (2) staff CVs with domain depth at the delivery level; (3) proprietary frameworks adapted to DC-specific failure modes; (4) understanding of client regulatory and commercial context; (5) embedded specialist subcontractor networks. Red flags: new vertical announced without corresponding hires; generic case studies with vertical vocabulary appended; inability to specify how methodology differs by vertical; no references in target sector.

**For Metier:** The public website has zero DC framing, but internal docs show genuine methodology adaptation (VPU→DC concept selection, KS2→FID readiness, MC simulation→CAPEX uncertainty). The depth is real but invisible externally.

## Section: Framework

### PM/strategy consultancy service taxonomy
Seven standard categories: (1) Strategy Advisory — directional counsel, business case development; (2) PMO/Program & Project Management — embedded delivery; (3) Owner's Engineer / PMC — outward-facing technical representation; (4) Procurement & Contract Advisory — contract strategy, tender management, claims risk; (5) Competence Development & Training — methodology certification, onboarding; (6) Digital/Technology Advisory — PMIS, BIM, DCIM, dashboards; (7) Portfolio Governance — stage-gate models, investor reporting. PMO and OE/PMC represent 50–70% of revenue in capital-intensive practices. Strategy opens doors; PMO retains clients for years.

### DC transferability by service category
- **Owner's Engineer / PMC — HIGH**: Structurally required by all non-technical owners entering DCs.
- **Project Execution & PM — HIGH**: Universal need; DC operators rarely carry internal PM capability.
- **Procurement & Contract Advisory — HIGH**: Long-lead equipment, complex scope-split decisions, security-clearance constraints make this harder than most sectors.
- **Portfolio Governance — HIGH**: Investor-mandated milestone reporting is non-discretionary and repeatable across every funded operator.
- **Strategy Advisory — MEDIUM**: High-value but episodic; requires DC sector credentialing.
- **Early-Phase Advisory — MEDIUM**: Metier's VPU/PDRI framework transfers well; technology choices need an engineering overlay.
- **Competence Development — MEDIUM**: Real demand but a follow-on, not entry point.
- **Digital Advisory — LOW**: Narrow applicability; strong specialist competition.

## Section: Expert Simulation

### Website capability signals
Most reliable signals (in order): (1) Case studies with operational specificity — dates, named counterparts, honest problem descriptions; (2) Named senior bios with checkable prior employers; (3) Methodology descriptions that reveal firm-specific process overlays, not just standard frameworks; (4) Third-party-audited accreditations. Least reliable: unsourced statistics, broad service line claims, unattributed testimonials. Sophisticated buyer test: can the claim be falsified? Does vocabulary match the domain? Is pedigree traceable?

### Under-represented capabilities on websites
Six categories typically invisible: (1) NDA-bound sector depth; (2) delivery-heavy capability undersold behind advisory framing; (3) sub-service specialisations hidden under generic labels; (4) individual partner expertise not codified; (5) volume/scale signals absent; (6) regulatory depth obscured for sector-neutral positioning. For new vertical entry: firms mistakenly lead with strategy/maturity assessments (long sales cycles, require pre-existing trust). What gets traction: bounded, high-stakes, near-term problems — owner's engineer roles, procurement support for long-lead equipment, schedule recovery. **Implication for Metier: lead with owner's engineer and procurement support, not strategy or governance frameworks.**

## Section: Failure Analysis

### DC applicability criteria for PM services
Three core criteria: (1) **Technical complexity** — MEP as product, not shell; design lock-in points; commissioning sequencing logic. (2) **Client type differentiation** — hyperscalers (integrate into their PMO), colo operators (revenue-sensitive phased delivery), PE-backed developers (milestone-gated capital, investor-grade reporting). Claiming all three without differentiation is a credibility gap. (3) **Procurement norms** — the 40–80 week long-lead equipment problem (transformers, switchgear, generators, UPS) and EPC vs. EPCM choice as the structural test of DC procurement understanding.

## Section: Applicability Assessment

### DC-ready delivery adaptations

**Owner's Engineer:** Must understand MEP hierarchy; own the commissioning programme; track long-lead equipment from contract award; understand nettilknytning/konsesjon processes in Norway.

**Project Execution & PM:** DC schedules are commissioning-driven (reverse planning from energisation date). Phase delivery planning essential (live phases alongside construction). Cost structure: ~40–50% equipment, not construction.

**Procurement & Contract Advisory:** Package strategy is fundamentally different — civil shell + MEP (electrical/mechanical split) + IT fit-out. Security-cleared contractor pool is severely restricted for high-security operators. Long-lead procurement must run ahead of contractor procurement.

**Portfolio Governance:** Track MW commissioned and revenue-ready, not just cost/schedule percentages. EU Taxonomy/ESG metrics embedded from day one. Multi-site interdependency (Site A delay affects Site B financing drawdown). CAPEX approval thresholds aligned to PE governance provisions.

**Strategy Advisory:** Requires DC sector knowledge — hyperscaler demand dynamics, Norwegian power market (NordPool, NO1–NO5 zone differentials), planning/regulatory processes.

**Early-Phase Advisory:** VPU/PDRI process transfers. Adaptation: embed DC technology choices (cooling topology, Tier, redundancy architecture, power density). Needs engineering overlay.

**Competence Development:** PM methodology training transfers. Adaptation: DC-specific modules for MEP package management, commissioning planning, equipment procurement, security compliance.

### Gap Analysis Summary

**What the website is missing:** All DC framing. A DC operator browsing metier.no finds no evidence of DC capability.

**What internal docs have that the website doesn't:** DC-specific service framing for all 8 service lines; 40+ additional granular services with competitive positioning; services like Uncertainty Analysis, KS2, Lender's Technical Advisory — all strong Metier capabilities invisible externally.

**What Metier cannot provide (structural gaps):**
- DC facility design (cooling/power engineering — needs Norconsult or Granlund)
- Proprietary DC cost benchmarking database
- Claims & dispute resolution
- Grid connection engineering
- Fire protection engineering
- Security clearance advisory

### Key Conclusions

1. Metier has genuine, high-quality capabilities for 6 of 8 identified DC service needs.
2. The public website creates an invisibility problem — GTM requires direct outreach.
3. Three natural entry services: Owner's Engineer, Project Execution & PM, Contract & Procurement.
4. Metier needs an engineering partner (Norconsult) for any service requiring DC-specific design.
5. The strongest competitive differentiation: Uncertainty Analysis / KS2 / FID readiness — these are Metier-signature services with no direct competitor offering the same methodology.
