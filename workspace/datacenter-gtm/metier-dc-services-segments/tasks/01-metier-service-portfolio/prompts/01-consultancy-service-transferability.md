# Prompt 01: consultancy-service-transferability

> **Prompt:** When assessing a consultancy's portfolio for a new vertical like data centers, what signals indicate genuine transferability?

---

## Genuine Transferability vs. Superficial Relabelling

### The Core Distinction

When a management or project consultancy enters a new vertical, the critical question is whether their underlying delivery capability transfers — or whether they have simply added a new industry name to existing brochure copy. The difference matters enormously to a sophisticated buyer: a relabelled generalist has surface-level vocabulary but lacks the situational judgment that comes from repeated execution in the specific context.

Genuine transferability rests on three things: **transferable process**, **transferable risk literacy**, and **personnel depth**. Superficial relabelling provides only vocabulary.

---

### Indicators of Genuine Capability Transfer

**1. Evidence of repeated execution in structurally similar projects**

The strongest signal is a portfolio of completed projects in adjacent sectors with comparable complexity vectors — not just project size, but the same types of risk exposure. For project management consultancies, data centers share structural characteristics with pharmaceutical cleanroom construction, semiconductor fabs, large grid-scale energy infrastructure, and mission-critical government facilities. A consultancy that has managed multiple pharmaceutical cleanroom builds has deep experience with stringent commissioning standards, parallel MEP workstreams, and owner's engineer relationships — all of which transfer directly to data center construction. Conversely, a firm with only commercial office or residential portfolio experience lacks this structural similarity regardless of how they label it.

**Concrete indicator:** Named case studies with enough detail to verify scope, complexity, and client outcomes — not generic testimonials. Verifiable references from clients in analogous sectors willing to speak to execution quality.

**2. Staff CVs with domain depth, not just domain exposure**

Genuine capability lives in people, not brochures. A firm that can name individuals with 5+ years of hands-on project delivery in comparable sectors — who can speak to specific technical challenges they navigated — is demonstrably different from a firm whose "data center practice" is staffed by generalists who attended a DC industry conference.

**Concrete indicator:** Identifiable project managers and technical leads on the team (not just partners) who have held client-side or PMO roles on comparable capital projects. LinkedIn profiles and CVs showing sustained engagement in the domain, not a single project used to justify a new practice label.

**3. Proprietary frameworks adapted to domain-specific risk**

Firms that genuinely execute in a new vertical develop frameworks that reflect its particular failure modes. In data centers, critical failure modes include long-lead equipment procurement (transformers, generators, UPS systems with 12–52 week lead times), parallel commissioning of IT and building infrastructure, power availability risk, and phased delivery against tenant pre-commitments. A consultancy with genuine DC experience will have adapted its risk registers, procurement checklists, and schedule logic to these specifics. A relabelled generalist uses generic frameworks with DC terminology inserted.

**Concrete indicator:** Ask to see a sample procurement plan or risk register used on a comparable project. Does it reflect the actual risk landscape of the sector (e.g., generator lead times, MEP package sequencing, commissioning interdependencies) — or is it a generic capital project template with some new vocabulary?

**4. Demonstrated understanding of the client's regulatory and commercial context**

In Norway, data center construction involves specific regulatory considerations: NSM frameworks for high-security facilities, konsesjon requirements for large power consumers, environmental impact assessments (konsekvensutredning), and grid connection timelines. A consultancy operating genuinely in this space will reference these by name and demonstrate experience navigating them. Similarly on the commercial side — understanding the economics of colocation leases, hyperscaler off-take agreements, and PE-driven capital structures signals real market literacy.

**Concrete indicator:** During a scoping conversation, can the firm's representative ask informed questions about your specific regulatory pathway and commercial structure — or do they only respond reactively with generic process descriptions?

**5. Network and ecosystem relationships**

Genuine execution in a vertical builds a network: relationships with specialist subcontractors, equipment vendors, inspection bodies, and regulatory counterparts. A firm that has delivered DC projects will know which MEP contractors in the Nordic market have the relevant technical certifications, which transformer suppliers have acceptable lead times, and which commissioning agents are credible. This embedded market knowledge is a natural byproduct of genuine execution and cannot be faked easily.

**Concrete indicator:** Can the firm provide specific vendor and contractor referrals relevant to your project — not just a framework for how to qualify vendors in general?

---

### Red Flags for Superficial Relabelling

**1. New vertical announced without corresponding hires**

If a consultancy announces a "data center practice" or "DC services" offering without any visible new hiring or acquisitions to support it, this is a strong signal of relabelling. Genuine vertical expansion typically requires bringing in at least a handful of domain specialists — either through targeted hiring or through acquisition of a smaller specialist firm. Watch for a gap between marketing claims and observable team composition.

**2. Generic case studies with vertical-specific vocabulary appended**

"We managed a 200,000 sqm construction project, and our approach applies equally to data centers" is the signature of relabelling. Genuine capability involves projects where the DC-specific challenges were actually encountered and overcome — not analogical claims that general project management applies everywhere (which, while technically true, misses the domain-specific judgment that buyers actually need).

**3. Inability to specify where their methodology differs by vertical**

Ask a firm directly: "How does your approach to procurement planning differ between a data center build and a commercial office fit-out?" If they cannot give a specific, detailed answer — naming the actual differences in contract packaging, equipment categories, lead times, and risk exposure — they are operating at the level of vocabulary, not execution knowledge.

**4. No references in the target sector**

A firm entering a new vertical genuinely should have some client references, even if limited — pilot engagements, advisory work, or adjacent-sector clients who can speak to the firm's adaptability. A firm that cannot produce a single reference with first-hand experience of their DC-relevant work should be treated cautiously.

**5. Undifferentiated capability claims**

"We can help with any major capital project" is not a DC value proposition. When every service offered sounds like it applies everywhere, it usually applies optimally nowhere. Genuine firms make specific claims about what they add in the specific context — not universal competence claims that by design avoid being tested.

### Analogous Examples from Professional Services Expansions

**Management consulting expansion into healthcare:** When McKinsey and BCG expanded into healthcare operations in the 1990s and 2000s, the genuine capability transfer came from bringing in clinical operational experts (physician-administrators, former hospital executives) alongside their generalist consultants. Firms that simply applied their standard operations playbooks without clinical literacy produced generic recommendations that often failed in implementation. The sustainable practices were built on people with dual expertise — not rebranded strategy decks.

**Engineering consultancies moving from oil & gas to renewables:** Firms like Arup and Atkins transferred genuine capability from offshore oil and gas into large-scale wind and grid infrastructure because the underlying engineering complexity vectors — subsurface risk, high-consequence failure modes, complex multi-contractor interfaces — were structurally similar. Pure rebranding firms tried to enter renewable energy with teams whose actual experience was in commercial buildings, and their lack of situational judgment showed in project execution failures.

**IT consultancies entering cybersecurity:** In the late 2000s, most major IT consultancies added "cybersecurity practice" to their portfolios. The firms that built genuine capability hired former intelligence community and defense personnel, developed proprietary threat intelligence frameworks, and invested in dedicated R&D. The relabellers added cybersecurity to their service line pages and trained existing IT consultants on general security frameworks — with predictably weaker client outcomes.

The pattern across all these cases: genuine vertical expansion requires a minimum viable investment in domain-specific human capital and adapted delivery frameworks. There is no shortcut.

### What a DC Operator or Procurement Team Should Look for When Qualifying a Consultancy

**1. Portfolio specificity test**

Request case studies with enough detail to evaluate: What was the capacity (MW)? What was the contract structure (EPC, EPCM, CM)? What were the primary risk events encountered and how were they resolved? What were the commissioning milestones and were they met? Generic "we have delivered infrastructure projects" responses should trigger follow-up scrutiny.

**2. Reference quality over reference quantity**

One reference from a DC operator whose project was delivered successfully is worth ten generic testimonials. In procurement qualification, ask for references specifically from DC operators or mission-critical facility clients — and call them. Ask specifically about what the consultancy did when things went wrong, not just when things went smoothly.

**3. Team CV review**

Request CVs for the individuals who would actually be assigned to your engagement. Senior partners who have one DC advisory engagement in their biography but whose actual depth is in another sector are a risk when delivery relies on their judgment. Look for evidence of sustained engagement in analogous environments — multiple projects, increasing responsibility, client-side and PMO-side roles.

**4. Fee structure and team composition as revealed preference**

A consultancy that prices a DC engagement like a generic project management retainer, with a team composition identical to what they would deploy on a retail fit-out, is signalling that they do not actually see your engagement as requiring domain-specific capability. Genuine DC specialists command premium rates for specific roles (commissioning managers, MEP cost controllers, Owner's Engineers with DC experience) and will explain why those roles are justified.

**5. Questions they ask you**

Perhaps the strongest signal of genuine versus superficial capability is the quality of a firm's discovery questions. A firm with genuine DC experience will ask about your power procurement timeline, your phasing strategy relative to tenant pre-commitments, your contractor clearance requirements, and your investor milestone gates. A relabelled generalist will ask about project scope, budget, timeline, and stakeholders — the same questions they ask every client.

---

### Synthesis: The Transferability Threshold

Genuine service transferability in professional services requires three conditions to be met simultaneously:

1. **Structural similarity** in the underlying work — the complexity vectors, risk types, and execution dynamics must be comparable to the firm's existing experience base.
2. **Human capital investment** — domain-specific expertise must exist in the team that will actually deliver, not just in senior advisors who lend their names to the practice.
3. **Adapted methodology** — the firm's frameworks must have been tested and refined in the new domain, not simply relabelled.

When a firm meets all three, vertical expansion produces genuine client value. When only one or two are present, the firm is operating at the edge of competence and clients bear the learning cost. When none are present, the firm is relabelling — and a sophisticated buyer should qualify them accordingly or price the engagement to reflect the embedded learning curve.
