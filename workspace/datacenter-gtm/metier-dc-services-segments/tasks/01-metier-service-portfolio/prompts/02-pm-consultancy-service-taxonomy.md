# Prompt 02: pm-consultancy-service-taxonomy

> **Prompt:** What are the main service categories for a PM/strategy consultancy?

---

## Standard Taxonomy: Services of a Project Management & Strategy Consultancy

A mature PM/strategy consultancy organizes its work into seven core service categories. The boundaries between categories are porous in practice — a single engagement often spans several — but the taxonomy is stable across the industry and is recognized by clients, procurement functions, and analysts alike.

---

### 1. Strategy Advisory

**What it involves:** High-level counsel delivered to C-suite and board on direction, investment priorities, and organizational positioning. Services include: portfolio and program strategy, market entry and feasibility analysis, business case development, operating model design, and investment decision frameworks. The deliverable is typically a structured recommendation or decision brief rather than a plan for execution.

**Tier 1 vs. Tier 3:** Tier-1 firms (McKinsey, BCG, Bain, or specialist infrastructure boutiques) bring proprietary benchmarks, cross-sector pattern recognition, and a partner network that adds credibility in boardrooms and with investors. Tier-3 generalists often repackage the client's own information back to them without adding external reference points. The gap shows most clearly when the strategy needs to hold up under investor scrutiny.

**What clients value most:** Independence and credibility. Clients hire strategy advisors partly to provide external validation for decisions already being considered — and partly to challenge assumptions they cannot challenge internally. Speed of synthesis and quality of the final narrative matter as much as analytical rigor.

---

### 2. PMO / Program & Project Management

**What it involves:** Establishing and running Project Management Offices, embedding project managers and directors into client organizations, managing schedules and budgets, coordinating multi-workstream programs, and providing progress reporting to steering committees. Ranges from setting up a PMO framework (methods, tools, governance) to fully staffing and running it.

**Tier 1 vs. Tier 3:** Tier-1 PMO practices (e.g., Turner & Townsend, Arcadis, or Metier in the Nordics) bring standardized methodologies validated across hundreds of capital projects, senior project directors with genuine delivery track records, and the bench depth to staff sudden surges. Tier-3 firms often provide individual contractors without a unifying method — the "PMO" exists in name only and quality depends entirely on the individual placed.

**What clients value most:** Reliability under pressure. A PMO is judged not when everything runs smoothly but when a critical path slips, a contractor defaults, or an investor demands a revised baseline at 48 hours' notice. Clients value consultancies whose people have been in those situations before and know what to do.

---

### 3. Owner's Engineer / PMC (Project Management Consultancy)

**What it involves:** Acting as the owner's technical representative throughout design and construction. The Owner's Engineer (OE) or PMC reviews contractor deliverables, manages interfaces between packages, represents the owner in design reviews and site meetings, oversees quality assurance, administers change orders, and monitors commissioning. Unlike a PMO, which is internally focused, the OE faces outward — toward contractors and suppliers.

**Tier 1 vs. Tier 3:** The distinction is domain depth. A strong OE practice in data centers (or oil & gas, or infrastructure) has engineers who have been on the other side — who have worked for EPC contractors and understand where corners get cut. They know what to look for in a progress claim or a design submission. Tier-3 providers often lack this technical depth and default to process-following rather than substantive review.

**What clients value most:** Technical credibility with contractors and independent judgment on the owner's behalf. Clients — especially financial investors building assets for the first time — need someone who will catch problems early, not ratify contractor claims. The highest-value OE engagements often involve pushing back on contractors on the client's behalf, which requires both technical knowledge and professional confidence.

---

### 4. Procurement & Contract Advisory

**What it involves:** Supporting the full sourcing lifecycle for capital projects: contract strategy (EPC, EPCM, CM, design-build), procurement planning, tender preparation and evaluation, supplier qualification, contract negotiation, and ongoing contract administration including claims management. Also covers framework agreements for repeat procurement.

**Tier 1 vs. Tier 3:** Tier-1 procurement advisors bring contract law knowledge, claims experience from prior disputes, and market intelligence on what comparable contracts actually contain. They can advise on risk allocation in contract terms — not just fill in a template. Tier-3 providers often lack claims experience and treat procurement as a compliance exercise rather than a risk management one.

**What clients value most:** Risk allocation clarity and supplier market knowledge. Clients want to know what risks they are retaining versus passing to contractors, and whether the terms they are offering will attract quality bidders or drive them away. Procurement advice that saves a client from a poorly structured contract can be worth multiples of the advisory fee.

---

### 5. Competence Development & Training

**What it involves:** Structured learning programs in project management methodologies (PMI/PMP, PRINCE2, IPMA), procurement, scheduling, leadership, and increasingly agile/hybrid delivery. Delivered as open enrollment, corporate cohorts, or embedded academies. Includes certification preparation, new-hire onboarding programs, and tailored workshops anchored to a client's actual projects.

**Tier 1 vs. Tier 3:** The differentiator is integration with delivery. Training programs run by firms that also do PMO and OE work bring current, real-world project examples into the classroom. Tier-3 training providers often deliver generic content disconnected from the client's specific context, leading to poor transfer of learning to the job. Accreditation partnerships (e.g., ATO status for PRINCE2) also signal quality.

**What clients value most:** Practical applicability and measurable competency uplift. Clients running fast-growing project organizations need training that visibly improves delivery outcomes — not box-ticking for HR. The best programs are co-designed with the client's PM leadership and calibrated to actual project challenges.

---

### 6. Digital / Technology Advisory

**What it involves:** Helping organizations select, implement, and integrate digital tools for project and portfolio management: PMIS platforms (Procore, Oracle Primavera Cloud, Bentley), BIM integration, document control systems, DCIM (for operators), custom dashboards for portfolio visibility, and data analytics capabilities. Also covers IT/enterprise architecture advisory and agile coaching for digital product development.

**Tier 1 vs. Tier 3:** Tier-1 digital practices combine tool knowledge with process design — they understand how a PMIS must be configured to reflect the client's governance model, not just how to install software. They also bring change management capability, because digital tool adoption fails more often due to people factors than technical ones. Tier-3 providers often implement tools in isolation from the surrounding process, producing systems that are technically functional but organizationally unused.

**What clients value most:** Integration and adoption. A dashboard that no one uses is worthless. Clients value advisors who design for adoption from the start — who understand that a field engineer in a hard hat and a CFO reviewing KPIs have different needs from the same data.

---

### 7. Portfolio Governance

**What it involves:** Enterprise-level oversight frameworks for organizations managing multiple simultaneous projects. Services include: portfolio governance design (decision rights, stage-gate models, investment committee structures), benefit realization tracking, resource allocation models, risk aggregation across a portfolio, and reporting frameworks that translate project-level data into board-level intelligence.

**Tier 1 vs. Tier 3:** Governance design requires understanding how boards and investment committees actually make decisions — not just what governance textbooks recommend. Tier-1 firms bring experience designing governance for PE-backed portfolios, regulated industries, and complex multi-stakeholder programs where the governance structure must also satisfy investor covenants and reporting obligations. Tier-3 providers often produce governance documentation that is process-complete but impractical — stage gates that cannot be navigated in real timelines, reporting templates that generate data no one reads.

**What clients value most:** Clarity of accountability and investor-grade reporting. The moment a PE investor or institutional backer requires portfolio-level visibility, the quality of the governance framework becomes a commercial issue — not just an internal management one. Clients value consultancies who have designed governance for that audience before.

---

## Cross-Cutting Observations

- **Revenue weight:** PMO/project management and Owner's Engineer/PMC are the highest-revenue categories in capital-intensive sectors, typically accounting for 50–70% of a full-spectrum consultancy's revenue. Procurement and portfolio governance are next. Training and digital are margin-enhancing adjacencies.
- **Relationship depth:** Strategy advisory opens doors; PMO and OE retain clients for years. The consultancies with the strongest long-term client relationships are those that move fluidly from strategy into execution.
- **Tier differentiation in practice:** The gap between tier 1 and tier 3 is least visible during scoping (everyone presents well) and most visible during delivery under stress — schedule compression, contractor claims, investor reporting crises. Procurement teams that select on price alone often discover the tier difference at exactly the wrong moment.
