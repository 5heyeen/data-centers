# Prompt 04: website-capability-signals

> **Prompt:** What website signals most reliably indicate consultancy capability depth?

---

## The Reliability Hierarchy

Not all website signals carry equal weight. A sophisticated buyer applies a mental hierarchy that distinguishes self-asserted claims from independently verifiable evidence and from signals that are costly to fake.

### Most Reliable Signals

**1. Case studies with operational specificity**
The most reliable signal is a case study that contains details a firm could only possess if it had actually done the work: contract value ranges, schedule overruns recovered, specific regulatory hurdles navigated, named subcontractors or client counterparts (where permitted), and honest accounts of what went wrong. Generic case studies ("we delivered on time and on budget") are marketing. Specific ones ("we recovered an 11-week delay on a 32MW shell-and-core build by restructuring the fit-out subcontracts and re-sequencing MEP commissioning") indicate genuine delivery experience. The presence of client-attributed quotes is a secondary check — a client who lets their name appear is implicitly endorsing the narrative.

**2. Named, senior-team biographies with verifiable prior employers**
Team bios that list specific roles at named prior firms, with tenure dates, are verifiable through LinkedIn and public records. A bio that says "20 years in project management" is unverifiable; one that says "Programme Director, Equinix EMEA 2016–2021, responsible for 14 fit-out projects across four countries" is cross-checkable and costly to fabricate. The senior team bio section is also a proxy for bench depth: a firm with five named partners and no mid-level staff visible is effectively a staffing vehicle, not a delivery organisation.

**3. Methodology descriptions that reveal proprietary process, not frameworks**
Firms with genuine methodology depth describe how they adapt standard frameworks to their specific domain. A description of PRINCE2 or PMI methodology is table stakes and reveals nothing — every firm claims these. What distinguishes genuine capability is a description of how the firm's own tools or process overlays sit on top of those frameworks: bespoke risk registers calibrated to specific project types, earned-value tracking adapted for design-led projects, or stage-gate governance customised for regulated industries. The presence of firm-specific templates, named tools, or described deviations from standard practice indicates that the firm has encountered the limits of generic frameworks and built around them.

**4. Accreditations that require third-party audit**
ISO 9001, ISO 14001, and sector-specific certifications (BREEAM assessor registration, CDM principal designer accreditation, etc.) require external audits with defined renewal cycles. They are harder to fake than self-stated credentials. However, accreditation alone is a threshold signal, not a differentiator — almost every credible firm carries them. Their absence is a red flag; their presence merely clears the bar.

### Medium-Reliability Signals

**Client lists** are of moderate reliability. A logo wall of recognisable clients creates a plausibility impression but is often outdated, may reflect a single small engagement, and cannot be independently verified in most cases. However, when client logos appear in combination with named case studies, the signal strengthens: the client is implicitly confirming the relationship by tolerating their brand's use.

**Award listings** are low-to-medium reliability. Industry awards (e.g., NCE awards, project management awards) involve third-party panels and carry some independent weight, but award categories are often self-selected and submissions are marketing documents. Awards from recognised professional bodies (APM, PMI) carry more weight than trade-press awards.

### Least Reliable Signals

**Unsourced statistics** ("over 500 projects delivered," "95% client satisfaction") are almost entirely unreliable. They are unaudited, undefined in scope, and trivially generated.

**Service line breadth claims** are negatively correlated with depth in many cases. A firm that lists 15 distinct service lines with equal prominence is almost certainly describing aspirational positioning rather than delivered capability. Real depth tends to concentrate, and firms with genuine specialisation tend to describe it in narrower terms with more technical language.

**Testimonials without attribution** are the weakest signal class. Any firm can write them.

## How a Sophisticated Buyer Distinguishes Capability from Marketing

A sophisticated buyer triangulates across three questions:

1. **Can the narrative be falsified?** Claims that contain checkable specifics (named clients, named projects, dates, scale) can be followed up. Claims that are abstract cannot. Preference goes to the specific and falsifiable.

2. **Does the vocabulary match the domain?** Genuine specialists use domain-specific language naturally: a real data centre PM talks about critical path on MEP commissioning, PUE targets embedded in design briefs, Tier certification dependencies on construction sequencing. Marketing copy tends toward generic terms ("complex infrastructure," "mission-critical environments"). A buyer who knows the domain will read the language closely.

3. **Is the team's pedigree traceable?** Senior bios should survive a LinkedIn audit. Prior employers should be credible delivery organisations. Career trajectories should show the kind of progression (delivery roles, not just advisory roles) that accumulates actual scar tissue from projects.

The combination of specific case studies, verifiable senior team backgrounds, and domain-precise language is the strongest composite signal. A firm that clears all three bars is unlikely to be performing capability it does not have.
