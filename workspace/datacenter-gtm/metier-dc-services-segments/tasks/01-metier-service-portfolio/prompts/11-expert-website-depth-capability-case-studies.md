# Prompt 11: expert-website-depth-capability-case-studies

> **Prompt:** What information on a consultancy's website most reliably indicates the depth of their capability — case studies, team bios, accreditations, client lists, or something else?

---

## Ranking Website Signals for Consultancy Capability Depth (Most to Least Reliable)

**1. Substantive Case Studies (Highest Reliability)**
Named client (or precisely described), specific problem, methodology used, quantified outcome. Hard to fabricate. Red flag: generic "A major Nordic infrastructure company..." with vague outcomes. Means the firm either lacks real wins or cannot get client permission — both meaningful.

**2. Team Bios and Credentials**
Highly informative because verifiable via LinkedIn. Look for: years of domain-specific experience, named projects/employers in prior roles, specialist certifications. Be suspicious of bios that are long on titles and short on specifics, or list certifications without noting when obtained.

**3. Methodology Documentation**
White papers, structured methodology pages, or downloadable frameworks. Underused as a signal because it requires effort and is hard to fake at depth. A consultancy that articulates *why* their approach works (not just *that* it works) demonstrates real intellectual capital.

**4. Accreditations and Partnerships**
ISO certifications, PMI REP status, software vendor partnerships. Useful floor — confirms minimum process maturity — but not differentiating. Almost every serious firm holds them. Necessary-but-not-sufficient.

**5. Client Logos (Lowest Reliability)**
Reveal nothing about scope, depth, or recency. A firm may have provided one training day to a large bank and legitimately display that logo. Cannot be audited without direct reference checks.

### What to Be Suspicious Of
- Testimonials without names, titles, or dates
- Award listings from bodies the firm itself sponsors or nominates to
- Heavy emphasis on firm size or office count rather than outcomes delivered
- Service pages that describe problems in generic terms with no evidence of *how* they are solved
- No acknowledgment of difficulty or complexity — real practitioners do not claim perfect outcomes

**The most reliable single indicator: a case study that a prospect could, in principle, call the named client to verify.**
