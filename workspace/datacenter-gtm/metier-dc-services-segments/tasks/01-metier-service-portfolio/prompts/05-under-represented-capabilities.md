# Prompt 05: under-represented-capabilities

> **Prompt:** What portfolio capabilities are typically invisible on websites, and what services get traction when entering new verticals?

---

## Part 1: What Is Invisible on Consultancy Websites — and Why

### The Structural Distortion

Consultancy websites are built for business development, not capability disclosure. They are optimised for initial contact and brand positioning, and specifically for avoiding disqualification. The dominant distortion is overselling breadth while underselling depth. A firm that shows generic language — "complex capital programmes," "major infrastructure projects" — keeps more doors open than one that signals narrow sector expertise. The cost is that genuine depth becomes invisible to the buyers who would value it most.

This creates a specific failure mode: the firm's website looks structurally identical to a dozen competitors. Differentiation only happens in conversation, through CV review, and reference calls — stages that many procurement processes never reach, and that buyers in new sectors may not know to trigger.

### Six Categories of Under-Represented Capability

**1. Confidential sector depth.** When a firm has done significant work for clients who cannot be named publicly — government agencies, defence-adjacent programmes, PE portfolio companies under NDA — that experience simply does not appear. The website may show zero evidence of a sector the firm has served for a decade.

**2. Operational and delivery capability vs. advisory framing.** Firms lead with strategy and advisory language because it positions them upstream and commands higher day-rates. But actual revenue and headcount is often concentrated in programme management, PMO delivery, owner's engineering, cost control, and construction oversight — hard, execution-heavy work that sounds unglamorous in marketing copy. The "delivery engine" that makes the firm viable is consistently undersold relative to the "advisory" language that frames it.

**3. Sub-service specialisations.** A website may list "project management" as a single line, hiding genuine depth in schedule risk analysis, earned value management, contract governance, commissioning management, or claims avoidance. These distinctions are largely irrelevant to a first-time buyer, but decisive to a sophisticated operator who needs a specific capability. The generic label conceals the differentiation.

**4. Individual partner and senior practitioner expertise.** Partners often carry capabilities the firm has never formally codified as a service line. A partner who spent fifteen years in energy infrastructure brings knowledge that the firm markets generically as "capital projects." That knowledge is real but invisible until a conversation surfaces it. Firms are poor at converting individual expertise into published, searchable capability signals.

**5. Volume and scale of delivery.** Websites rarely communicate how many projects a firm runs concurrently, total CAPEX supervised, or the typical team size deployed on a major programme. These are precisely the indicators a sophisticated operator uses to assess whether the firm can handle a large or complex engagement. The absence of scale signals creates underestimation.

**6. Regulatory and compliance depth.** Firms operating in tightly regulated environments — nuclear decommissioning, defence, critical infrastructure, high-security data centres — often use deliberately generic language to maintain positioning flexibility across sectors. The regulatory expertise is real; the public signal is deliberately suppressed.

### Organizational Blind Spots as a Compounding Factor

Beyond strategic concealment and client confidentiality, some capabilities are invisible because the firm itself has not codified them. A capability delivered by one or two practitioners, never written up as a methodology, never branded, and never quoted in a proposal without that individual's involvement is genuinely invisible externally — and fragile internally. Firms entering new verticals often discover that their most transferable capabilities are precisely the ones they have not named.

A related blind spot: temporal misalignment. Websites often reflect the firm's founding thesis or a five-year-old strategic pivot, not the current capability mix. A firm that built its reputation in public sector programme management but has spent the last four years on private infrastructure finance still shows public sector imagery and language. The capability mix has shifted; the website has not.

### Implications for Assessing Portfolio Completeness from Public Sources

Public sources can tell you what a firm is willing to be seen doing, at the level of abstraction that maximises their addressable market. They cannot, by themselves, tell you:
- Where actual revenue is concentrated
- Whether listed services are delivered by dedicated specialists or stretched generalists
- What the firm has done under NDA in adjacent sectors
- How sophisticated the methodology actually is behind generic service labels

Reliable supplementary signals — in decreasing order of accessibility — are: job postings (what the firm is actively hiring for); LinkedIn employee role distributions (where specialist titles cluster); case study depth and outcome specificity (core capabilities generate measurable results); named methodologies or branded tools (peripheral services are described generically); and reference calls (the single most reliable source).

For Metier specifically: the risk is that deep, repeatable capability in structured project delivery — schedule governance, owner's engineering, cost control, procurement strategy — is communicated in language generic enough to be dismissed by a DC operator screening for sector-specific experience. The capability may be fully transferable; the website may give no signal that Metier understands DC-specific execution challenges.

## Part 2: What Firms Lead With vs. What Gets Traction in New Verticals

### The Common Error: Leading with Abstract, Relationship-Stage-Inappropriate Services

When professional services firms enter a new vertical, they default to their core competency framing — strategy, maturity assessments, transformation roadmaps, organizational design. These services require significant pre-existing trust, assume the buyer is willing to acknowledge inadequacy at a strategic level, and carry long sales cycles. In a new vertical, the firm has no trust capital. Presenting abstract advisory to a buyer who has never heard of you is a near-certain path to a polite decline.

The second error is pitching to the wrong buyer. Firms naturally gravitate toward C-suite conversations because that is where strategy discussions live. But in capital-intensive verticals like data center development, the relevant budget and immediate pain often sits with the project director, construction manager, or COO — not the CEO. The entry path is through an operational problem, not a strategic one.

### What Actually Gets Traction: Bounded, High-Stakes, Near-Term Problems

Traction in new verticals almost always comes from engaging with a concrete, time-boxed problem where the cost of failure is visible. In data center development specifically, the services that generate early traction are:

- **Owner's engineer and owner's representation** during design and construction: a concrete, scoped role with clear deliverables, where the buyer does not need to trust the firm's overall capabilities — only their ability to protect the owner's interests in a specific phase
- **Procurement strategy and contract packaging** for long-lead equipment: generators, transformers, cooling systems, and UPS carry 40–80 week lead times, and operators without construction DNA consistently underestimate this; the stakes are immediate and the problem is specific
- **Schedule recovery and construction programme management**: when a build is running late, a firm that can stabilise the programme is valuable immediately, without any sector track record required
- **Commissioning planning and management**: a bounded phase with high consequence — getting commissioning wrong delays revenue and, for PE-backed operators, threatens the exit timeline

These services share a common structure: they are scoped, the outcome is measurable, the timeline is defined, and the cost of failure is clear to the buyer before the engagement starts. The buyer does not need to take a position on the firm's general capabilities — they are buying a specific result.

### Why Some Services Have Longer Sales Cycles

Services with long sales cycles in new verticals share predictable characteristics:

- They require the buyer to trust the firm's judgment over their own, which requires prior relationship
- They are upstream of visible problems — strategy and governance work prevents future pain rather than solving present pain, and "preventing future pain" is a hard sell when present pain is absorbing all available attention
- They implicitly ask the buyer to acknowledge that their current approach is inadequate, which creates social friction with internal stakeholders
- The output is a document or framework rather than a concrete project outcome, making it harder to justify to a board or investor

Portfolio governance, competence development, and strategy advisory are all genuinely valuable — but they are earned services, not entry services. They follow a track record, not precede it.

### The Underlying Dynamic and Implication for Metier in DC

New-vertical entry requires credibility before scope. Buyers in specialised sectors are skeptical of generalists, and data center development has a culture of sector-specific expertise that is particularly hostile to generalist framing.

The reliable entry sequence is: (1) win an engagement on something specific, technical, and time-boxed; (2) deliver visibly, generating a concrete outcome the client can point to; (3) use that track record as the credibility foundation for advisory, governance, or strategic conversations. Attempting to skip step 1 and open with step 3 — however rational from a margin perspective — reliably fails.

For Metier in data centers: the entry services are owner's engineer and procurement support on a specific project. The pitch is not "we are a strategy and governance firm that also does execution." The pitch is "we can protect your interests on this specific procurement challenge or construction programme" — and the strategic advisory relationship follows if delivery is strong.
