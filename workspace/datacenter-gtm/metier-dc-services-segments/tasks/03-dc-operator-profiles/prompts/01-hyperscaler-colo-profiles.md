# Chunk 01: hyperscaler-colo-profiles
> **Topic:** Hyperscaler and colo operator profiles as Metier clients
---

## 1. Hyperscalers (Google, Microsoft, AWS, Meta)

**How they buy.** Hyperscalers build massive internal machinery — construction program management offices, procurement centers of excellence, global supply chain teams. Microsoft, Google, and AWS each run capital programs exceeding €10B/year and staff accordingly. They develop proprietary design standards (Microsoft's Generation 5 campus, Google's data center playbook) that reduce reliance on external design consultants. External advisors are brought in selectively, mainly to fill specialist gaps or surge capacity, not to lead.

**What they keep in-house.** Site selection, concept design, grid negotiations, procurement strategy, EPC contractor management, and commissioning are all core internal competencies. They typically own relationships with Skanska, AECOM, Turner, and Jacobs directly and do not need third parties to manage those relationships.

**What they outsource.** Specialist studies (geotechnical, environmental permitting, grid capacity assessments), local regulatory navigation, site supervision on specific packages, and surge PM capacity during peak construction phases. In Norway specifically, hyperscalers need local partners who understand Norges vassdrags- og energidirektorat (NVE) grid concession processes and municipal zoning — but this is compliance support, not strategic advisory.

**Decision-makers.** Global real estate and construction teams (typically VP/Director level based in Dublin, Amsterdam, or US HQ) hold budget and framework contract authority. Local country managers have influence over site selection and community relations, but rarely own advisory spend. Framework contracts are competed globally — Metier would need to qualify as an approved vendor, a multi-year process.

**Metier opportunity: very low.** Hyperscalers do not need an external owner's engineer or PMC — they are the owner's engineer. The realistic entry point is as a local specialist subcontractor on a specific permitting or regulatory task, but margins are thin, scopes are narrow, and procurement processes are designed to minimize advisor dependency. **Not a target segment for Metier.**

## 2. Colocation Operators

**Profile.** Colo operators (Green Mountain, DigiPlex/Stack Infrastructure, Lefdal Mine Datacenter, Bulk Infrastructure, Colobridge) sell power, space, and cooling to enterprise and cloud tenants. Their capital programs are smaller than hyperscalers but structurally similar — they build shells, then fit out per customer spec. Norwegian colos benefit from competitive power prices, renewable energy credentials, and natural cooling (fjord water, mountain air), which drives international customer interest and expansion pressure.

**Project challenges.** Colo expansions are capital-intensive against uncertain demand — operators must often break ground before anchor tenants are signed (speculative build). This creates schedule pressure (customer commits trigger SLA clocks), cost discipline requirements (CAPEX must pencil at competitive $/kW pricing), and complexity in managing civil, power, and cooling workstreams in parallel. Green Mountain's Rennesøy site and Lefdal's mine conversion both required bespoke engineering solutions — long-lead equipment, unusual civil environments — that stretch thin internal teams. Stack/DigiPlex operates multi-country portfolios where governance and procurement consistency across sites is a real challenge.

**Pain points where external advisory creates value.**
- **Owner's Engineer / PMC:** Most Norwegian colos are commercially led — founders and executives from IT, telecom, or real estate, not construction. When they build their third or fourth site, the team is often still sized for the first. An external OE provides contractor oversight without permanently growing the headcount.
- **Procurement & Contract Strategy:** Splitting civil, MEP, and cooling packages correctly determines cost and schedule. First-time or rapidly scaling operators get this wrong. Metier can define contract packaging, run competitive tendering, and manage bid evaluations.
- **Portfolio Governance:** Operators like Stack (multi-country) or Bulk (multiple Norwegian sites) need standardized stage-gate reporting across projects. Investor and lender reporting requirements (DSCR covenants, ESG mandates) add burden that lean internal teams cannot absorb without a governance framework.
- **Early-Phase Advisory:** Site selection and phasing decisions (when to commit to next MW tranche) are high-stakes and often made without rigorous frameworks. Metier's VPU / front-end advisory is directly applicable here.

**Buying behavior.** Decisions are made by the CEO or VP of Construction/Development — typically a small leadership group. Procurement is informal: relationships, referrals from other operators, and reputation in the local construction market matter more than formal frameworks. Colo operators are cost-conscious (thin margins on kW pricing) but will pay for advisory that demonstrably reduces CAPEX risk or shortens time-to-revenue.

**Decision-makers to target.** VP Construction, VP Development, CFO (if the engagement touches investor reporting), and the CEO for strategic advisory. Board-level engagement is relevant when PE or infrastructure fund investors are involved (Stack is PE-backed; Bulk has institutional investors).

**Metier opportunity: high.** Norwegian colo operators are the primary target segment. They have real execution challenges, thin internal teams, PE or institutional capital requiring governance, and a track record of bespoke projects that benefit from experienced OE support. The Nordic market is small enough that Metier's local reputation travels — a strong reference from one colo operator opens doors to others.
