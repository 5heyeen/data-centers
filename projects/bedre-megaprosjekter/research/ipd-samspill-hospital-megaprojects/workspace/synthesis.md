# Synthesis: IPD/Samspill Feasibility for Hospital Megaprojects (~20B NOK, >100,000 m²)

**Scope:** Is an IPD (Integrated Project Delivery) / "samspill" contract model feasible for hospital projects larger than 100,000 m² with a budget of approximately 20 billion NOK, and if so what contract model structure would make it workable?
**Date:** 2026-04-25
**Tasks synthesised:** 6 (processed in 3 chunks of 2 tasks each)

---

## Executive Answer

Multi-party IPD / IPL (Integrert Prosjektleveranse) is **feasible** for Norwegian hospital megaprojects at ~20B NOK, and legal permissibility has already been established in practice. Both Statsbygg and Sykehusbygg HF have signed multi-party IPL contracts under Norwegian public procurement law (LOA/FOA), and the Tønsberg Project (2015–2021) demonstrated that Norwegian hospital sector participants can operate within shared risk/gain structures adapted from US IPD templates. The core legal question — is multi-party IPL permissible for a public helseforetak? — is answered: yes.

The scale challenge is structural, not legal. The largest Norwegian IPL contract in any sector is ~7B NOK (Statsbygg Livsvitenskapsbygget), and the largest confirmed Norwegian hospital IPL is ~1.5B NOK (Sykehusbygg SPH Øya at St. Olavs hospital, 2025). The research target of ~20B NOK is 3x above the national samspill ceiling and 12x above the hospital IPL ceiling. No single IPD or alliance contract anywhere in the world has been documented as covering a full hospital programme at 20B NOK scale in one agreement. The international evidence — most notably the Sutter Health CPMC Van Ness Campus ($2.1B USD, $150M under budget) — demonstrates that multi-party IPD can deliver at or above 20B NOK equivalent, but this was a single-building US project under US civil contract law, not a directly transferable model.

The contract model structure that makes large-scale IPL workable is a **programme of aligned IPL packages** rather than one monolithic agreement. Each major package (structural/råbygg, MEP/teknikk, medical equipment, digital infrastructure) would be delivered under a separate 3-party IPL agreement (owner + package contractor + package consultant), all governed by an overarching Programme Alliance Leadership Team with a shared contingency pool and cross-package interface management provisions. This structure mirrors what Sutter Health achieved over 8 years with 15 separate IPD projects — but compressed into a coordinated programme for a single hospital campus. Pain/gain sharing caps would need to be calibrated to ±5% of target cost per package (not the standard ±10%), given Norwegian contractor balance sheet constraints. Integrated Project Insurance (IPI) does not exist at this scale and should not be assumed. Mutual liability waivers — with Norwegian law-compliant carve-outs for gross negligence and wilful misconduct — are the only available mechanism.

The primary obstacles are not legal but governance: any megaproject-scale IPL in the Norwegian hospital sector must clear four sequential governance gates (helseforetak board, regional health authority, Ministry of Health/state loan framework, KS2 external quality assurance), each requiring the novel procurement model to be defensible against traditional alternatives on value-for-money grounds. The Norwegian traditional model's actual performance — confirmed 11–24% overruns at SUS2023 and OUS/Nye Rikshospitalet — provides the counterfactual baseline. Mjøssykehuset (~18–19B NOK, construction start planned 2028) has not yet selected a contract strategy and represents the most immediate window for applying IPL at or near megaproject scale.

## Key Findings (Cited)

### Finding 1: The Norwegian traditional hospital model has a structural cost-overrun problem

The Norwegian public hospital sector has a documented pattern of significant cost overruns using traditional multi-package split contracts. OUS/Nye Rikshospitalet and Nye Aker — the country's largest active hospital programme at 305,000 m² — escalated from a P50 estimate of NOK 36,045M (January 2021) to NOK 44,700M (December 2024), a pre-completion increase of +24%. The NRK projection for eventual outturn is "over 50 milliarder." SUS2023 Stavanger (125,000 m²) overran its approved budget of NOK 11,205M to NOK 12,475M (+11.3%), driven primarily by a severe mismatch between the 3% annual inflation assumption embedded in the contract and the 10–20% actual construction inflation experienced from 2021 onward. Both projects used traditional multi-package split contracts with no collaborative risk-sharing mechanism.

The positive counterexample is St. Olavs Hospital in Trondheim — the most important positive reference in Norwegian hospital megaproject history — which was delivered at NOK 12,465M against a cost ceiling of NOK 13,932M (−10.5%, ~NOK 1.5B under budget). The key differentiators were strong integrated governance (hospital + NTNU co-development), a phased delivery structure, and an experienced building committee, rather than a specific contract model. The contract model at St. Olavs is not confirmed in any accessible source.

Underlying these individual cases is a structural cause identified by NTNU's Concept research programme: "Deficiencies in the early phase of projects, before the decision to initiate the project was made, was the overarching main cause of large cost overruns in project implementation." — [Concept/NTNU research programme: https://www.ntnu.edu/concept]

The debt financing model amplifies overrun consequences: Norwegian hospital trusts borrow state loans to fund construction, meaning overruns translate directly into higher debt service from operating budgets. "Gjeldskrise for norske sykehus: Betaler milliarder i renter til staten" (Norwegian hospitals in debt crisis: Paying billions in interest to the state) — [NRK Rogaland: https://www.nrk.no/rogaland/gjeldskrise-for-norske-sykehus_-betaler-milliarder-i-renter-til-staten-1.17542482]

**Supporting quotes:**

### Finding 2: Multi-party IPL is already legal and in use in Norway — the permissibility question is resolved

Multi-party IPL (Integrert Prosjektleveranse) has been procured and signed by both Statsbygg (central government) and Sykehusbygg HF (the dedicated hospital construction body). The legal permissibility question — can a Norwegian helseforetak sign a multi-party IPL? — is answered by practice.

Sykehusbygg HF signed its first IPL contract for Senter for psykisk helse Øya (SPH) at St. Olavs hospital in 2025: "Senter for psykisk helse Øya (SPH) is the first project where Sykehusbygg HF uses Integrert prosjektleveranse (IPL), which is a relationship-based delivery model that can be considered a further development of total enterprise with collaboration." — [Byggeindustrien/bygg.no: https://www.bygg.no/tar-i-bruk-relasjonsbasert-modell-pa-sykehusbygg-prosjekt/1494483!/] The SPH Øya contract is approximately NOK 1.5B excluding VAT, forming part of a NOK 3.555B total framework. It is a three-party agreement between client, IPL contractor, and IPL advisor.

Statsbygg signed its first IPL for NTNU Campussamling P4 Musikk at approximately NOK 500M: "The contract value is approximately 500 million kroner...carried out as an Integrated Project Delivery (IPL-contract), which means the project is developed in a tripartite collaboration between Veidekke, Norconsult, and Statsbygg." — [Statsbygg/norskbyggebransje.no search snippet; Statsbygg project page: https://www.statsbygg.no/nyheter/statsbygg-har-signerte-sin-forts-ilp-kontrakt-med-entreprenor-veidekke-as-og-radgiver-norconsult-as-for-delprosjektet-p4-musikk-i-ntnu-campussamling]

The governing legal framework — LOA/FOA implementing EU Directive 2014/24/EU — does not mandate fixed-price contracts. It requires that the procurement procedure is competitive, not that the contract payment mechanism is fixed-price. Open-book, cost-reimbursable multi-party agreements are legally unproblematic once the team is selected through a competitive procedure. "Norway has adopted relevant EU legislation with respect to public procurement as part of the EEA Agreement...EU's law on public procurement has been incorporated into Norwegian law." — [CMS Expert Guide Norway: https://cms.law/en/int/expert-guides/cms-expert-guide-to-public-procurement/norway]

**Supporting quotes:**
- "Senter for psykisk helse Øya (SPH) is the first project where Sykehusbygg HF uses Integrert prosjektleveranse (IPL)...a three-party agreement between client, IPL contractor, and IPL advisor." — [Byggeindustrien/bygg.no: https://www.bygg.no/tar-i-bruk-relasjonsbasert-modell-pa-sykehusbygg-prosjekt/1494483!/]
- "I IPL, inngår byggherren, entreprenøren og rådgiverne en felles trepartsavtale med delt risiko og gevinst, med alle nøkkelaktørene involvert fra starten, organisert som et integrert team med felles ledelsesstruktur, åpne budsjetter og felles mål." — [Nye Veier IPL documentation, WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]

**Confidence:** High — both Sykehusbygg and Statsbygg IPL contracts are confirmed through direct reporting from Byggeindustrien and Statsbygg's own website. The legal framework analysis draws on CMS Expert Guide (reputable international law firm practitioner guide for Norway).

### Finding 3: The Norwegian samspill scale ceiling creates a 3x–12x gap to the research target

The largest Norwegian samspill/IPL contract in any sector is approximately NOK 7 billion: "Statsbygg går for samspill på Livsvitenskapsbygget" — a university research building at UiO Blindern with "total budget approximately NOK 7 billion." — [Tungt.no: https://www.tungt.no/article/view/614523/statsbygg_gar_for_samspill_pa_livsvitenskapsbygget] This is a totalentreprise med samspill (single main contractor + ECI collaboration phase), not a multi-party IPL.

The largest confirmed Norwegian hospital IPL contract is SPH Øya at ~NOK 1.5B (see Finding 2). The scale gap from SPH Øya to the research target of ~20B NOK is approximately 12x. The scale gap from the Norwegian samspill ceiling (7B NOK) to the research target is approximately 3x.

No samspill or IPL contract — in the hospital sector or any other sector — has been executed in Norway above ~7B NOK. At the research target scale of 20B NOK, a single monolithic samspill/IPL agreement is structurally unprecedented both in Norway and internationally for a hospital programme. The Norwegian samspill research community has acknowledged that the competence base for even current-scale samspill is thin: "Kompetansen på byggherresiden er variabel og til dels begrenset, og samspillskontrakter utvikles ofte etter beste evne av folk som ikke har egen erfaring med gjennomføring av samspill." (The competence on the owner side is variable and partly limited, and samspill contracts are often developed to the best of the ability of people who have no own experience of executing samspill.) — [Concept research/Marstrand search snippet, WebSearch 2026-04-25; see also Marstrand.no: https://marstrand.no/samspill/samspill-som-gjennomforingsmodell-utfordringer/]

**Supporting quotes:**
- "Statsbygg går for samspill på Livsvitenskapsbygget...total budget approximately NOK 7 billion" — [Tungt.no: https://www.tungt.no/article/view/614523/statsbygg_gar_for_samspill_pa_livsvitenskapsbygget]
- "Det eksperimenteres med et utall varianter – gode og dårlige." (A multitude of variants — good and bad — are being experimented with.) — [Concept/Marstrand research, WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]
- "Samspill krever mye av byggherreorganisasjonen når det gjelder kompetanse, bemanning og ressurser" — [Concept/Marstrand search snippet, WebSearch 2026-04-25]

### Finding 4: Sutter Health CPMC Van Ness is the strongest single international proof of multi-party IPD at target scale

The Sutter Health California Pacific Medical Center (CPMC) Van Ness Campus in San Francisco is the most relevant international comparator to the ~20B NOK hospital megaproject scale, having been delivered under a multi-party IPD agreement at $2.1 billion USD (~22–24B NOK at 2024 exchange rates): "Sutter announced at the 2018 LCI Congress that the project is $150 million under the original project budget." — [SmithGroup: https://www.smithgroup.com/perspectives/2018/making-lean-integrated-project-delivery-ipd-work]

The Van Ness Campus used a three-party Integrated Form of Agreement (IFOA) among Sutter Health (owner), SmithGroup (architect), and HerreroBoldt (contractor), with 240 design professionals working in a co-located "big room" environment. It is the largest documented single-building hospital project delivered under a multi-party IPD contract and the only confirmed case at or above the 20B NOK scale benchmark.

The Van Ness delivery is part of a broader Sutter Health portfolio demonstrating multi-party IPD at hospital scale: "During the period 2007 to 2015, Sutter delivered several large hospital projects and multiple smaller clinic projects on time, on budget and most importantly, with no compromise to the clinical operational vision for these facilities. This was a total of $1.5 billion across fifteen separate projects." — [Berkeley P2SL: https://p2sl.berkeley.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/Conwell-2012-08-29-P2SL-Presentation-Sutter-Health-DC.pdf]

The Norwegian transferability caveat is significant: the Sutter IFOA is a US civil contract law instrument, not directly applicable in the Norwegian public procurement framework (LOA/FOA). Every element — procurement procedure, multi-party agreement structure, liability waivers, insurance — would require material adaptation. The CPMC demonstrates feasibility of scale; it does not provide a transferable model.

**Supporting quotes:**
- "Sutter announced at the 2018 LCI Congress that the project is $150 million under the original project budget" — [SmithGroup: https://www.smithgroup.com/perspectives/2018/making-lean-integrated-project-delivery-ipd-work]
- "During the period 2007 to 2015, Sutter delivered several large hospital projects...on time, on budget...a total of $1.5 billion across fifteen separate projects." — [Berkeley P2SL: https://p2sl.berkeley.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/Conwell-2012-08-29-P2SL-Presentation-Sutter-Health-DC.pdf]
- "By employing integrated project delivery (IPD) and Lean construction methods, Sutter Health Eden Medical Center was completed on time and within budget...the first large complex project delivered under an IPD contract in the USA." — [WebSearch snippet referencing DPR/Structure Magazine, 2026-04-25]

**Confidence:** High for budget outcome (reported by SmithGroup at LCI Congress, a credible practitioner source). Medium for transferability — the US–Norway legal and procurement context difference is significant and well-established; Norwegian public procurement constraints are directly limiting factors.

### Finding 5: The multi-party contract is a necessary enabler for IPD — but not sufficient alone

The Tønsberg Project (44,500 m², ~NOK 2.7B, 2015–2021) is the only Norwegian hospital project to have implemented a full multi-party IPD contract. It was adapted from an American IPD template adjusted to Norwegian law. Research on the Tønsberg Project produced a key finding that reframes how the multi-party contract should be understood: "The interviewees did not see direct effects of the multiparty contract itself. However...the multiparty contract is necessary to enable implementation of other IPD elements, such as early involvement of key participants." — [MATEC Web of Conferences: https://www.matec-conferences.org/articles/matecconf/ref/2020/08/matecconf_eppm2018_07001/matecconf_eppm2018_07001.html]

The Tønsberg Project also produced important behavioural evidence on the limits of rapid IPD adoption: "Too many elements were attempted implemented at once, causing a tendency to fall back on traditional ways of doing things when the process lagged." — [Emerald Insight: https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/ci-03-2021-0042/full/html] This finding carries direct implications for a 20B NOK megaproject IPL: the more complex the project and the larger the number of parties and packages, the greater the risk of IPD principles being abandoned in practice during execution.

The IPD elements that the multi-party contract enables — and that must be actively built alongside the contract structure — include: early involvement of key participants, shared risk/gain incentives visible to all parties, Target Value Design discipline, co-location/Big Room collaboration, and open-book cost transparency.

**Supporting quotes:**
- "The multiparty contract is necessary to enable implementation of other IPD elements, such as early involvement of key participants." — [MATEC Web of Conferences: https://www.matec-conferences.org/articles/matecconf/ref/2020/08/matecconf_eppm2018_07001/matecconf_eppm2018_07001.html]
- "Too many elements were attempted implemented at once, causing a tendency to fall back on traditional ways of doing things when the process lagged." — [Emerald Insight: https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/ci-03-2021-0042/full/html]
- "The greatest benefit of sharing risk and reward reported in the Tønsberg project is the parties realizing they need to work toward the common goal, prioritizing the whole instead of individual performance." — [Emerald Insight: https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/ci-03-2021-0042/full/html]

**Confidence:** High — three peer-reviewed papers (ResearchGate/IGLC, MATEC Web of Conferences, Emerald Insight) document the Tønsberg Project. The "necessary but not sufficient" finding is from a critical perspective paper, not a promotional report.

### Finding 6: PPP at megaproject hospital scale has a consistent track record of failure in Nordic/European contexts

Three major Nordic and international hospital megaprojects delivered under PPP/DBFM contracts failed severely on cost and schedule, establishing a clear pattern:

- **New Karolinska Solna (Sweden):** SEK 14.5B contracted via PPP DBFM → lifecycle cost escalated to SEK 61B+; 4-year delay. One of the most studied hospital PPP failures in Europe.
- **Nyt OUH Odense (Denmark):** 275,000 m², DKK 6.9B budgeted → DKK 985M+ overrun, 5-year delay (2022 → 2027). Main contractor (Italian Condotte) went into receivership during construction. This is the most direct scale comparator to Mjøssykehuset by size and geography.
- **New Royal Adelaide Hospital (Australia):** AUD 1.7B contracted → ~AUD 2.3–2.77B (35% overrun), 1-year delay, $500M+ legal dispute. — [IPA/ResearchGate coverage, WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]

The pattern across all three: PPP DBFM transfers headline financial risk to a private consortium that cannot bear it at megaproject hospital scale; when the consortium fails or claims, the public sector absorbs the loss through legal dispute and renegotiation.

For Norway specifically, the PPP model is structurally incompatible with the Norwegian helseforetak financing model: state loans (used to fund hospital construction) cannot serve as the payment mechanism for availability payments to a PPP consortium. This removes PPP from the Norwegian option set entirely, independent of its international performance track record.

By contrast, the QEUH Glasgow (£842M, traditional model with Brookfield Multiplex) "was completed under budget and ahead of schedule" — [Multiplex: https://www.multiplex.global/projects/the-queen-elizabeth-university-hospital-the-royal-hospital-for-children/] — demonstrating that traditional contracts with strong client cost governance can also succeed, though at approximately 1/3 the target scale.

**Supporting quotes:**
- "The project was completed under budget and ahead of schedule by Brookfield Multiplex." — [Multiplex: https://www.multiplex.global/projects/the-queen-elizabeth-university-hospital-the-royal-hospital-for-children/]
- "The client and design team worked closely to establish an overall cost model and fixed budgets which were used to control and monitor the design." — [WSP: https://www.wsp.com/en-us/projects/queen-elizabeth-university-hospital-glasgow-uk]

**Confidence:** High for PPP failure pattern (New Karolinska and Nyt OUH are extensively documented in Scandinavian and European media). Medium for specific cost figures on Nyt OUH and New Karolinska (all primary sources returned 403; figures from search snippets). The structural incompatibility of PPP with Norwegian helseforetak financing is an inference, but a well-supported one (state loan framework incompatibility with availability payment structure).

### Finding 7: Competitive dialogue is the appropriate procurement procedure for megaproject-scale IPL

Norwegian public procurement law (FOA) provides four procedures relevant to complex hospital construction: restricted tender, competitive procedure with negotiation, competitive dialogue (konkurransepreget dialog), and innovation partnership. For multi-party IPL at 10–20B NOK scale, competitive dialogue is the most appropriate: it allows the contracting authority to discuss all aspects of the contract — including the IPL structure, risk/gain parameters, target cost methodology, and governance requirements — with shortlisted IPL teams before final offers are submitted.

Competitive dialogue in Norway is confirmed as legally available but practically underused: "Competitive Dialogue (CD) is a novel procurement procedure in Norway, with the NPRA having used the procedure in only six projects." — [Academia.edu, WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25] Users report that "contracting parties that have used the procedure have had various positive experiences, with findings having implications in improving project outputs, building better trust and cooperation between parties and better use of public money." — [Emerald Insight: https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/jopp-09-2022-0041/full/html]

The practical challenge at megaproject scale is owner capability: running a competitive dialogue process for a 20B NOK IPL team covering all disciplines (structural, MEP, technical, equipment) for a 100,000+ m² hospital would be among the most complex public procurement exercises ever conducted in Norway. This points directly to the indispensable role of Sykehusbygg HF as the experienced procurement manager, supported by specialist procurement advisers.

**Supporting quotes:**
- "Contracting parties that have used the procedure have had various positive experiences, with findings having implications in improving project outputs, building better trust and cooperation between parties and better use of public money." — [Emerald Insight: https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/jopp-09-2022-0041/full/html]
- "The negotiated procedure and competitive dialogue may be used when the contracting authority has a need that cannot be met without adapting solutions that already exist in the market, or when the procurement involves design or innovative solutions." — [CMS Expert Guide Norway/Anskaffelser.no WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]

**Confidence:** High for legal availability of competitive dialogue (confirmed in FOA). Medium for practical application at IPL megaproject scale — no Norwegian hospital has yet used competitive dialogue for an IPL contract; the NPRA road sector experience is the closest analogue.

### Finding 8: Integrated Project Insurance is not available at Norwegian megaproject hospital scale

Integrated Project Insurance (IPI), which collectively insures the client and all project partners against financial loss beyond the target cost plus pain-share, is the theoretically ideal risk mechanism for multi-party IPD. In practice it is not available: "IPI replaces liability-driven professional indemnity insurance (which requires proof of fault before responding) with financial loss cover where the actual construction cost above the target cost plus pain-share is insured." — [UK Government IPI FAQ: https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/5a7d6b0040f0b60a7f1aa665/Integrated_Project_Insurance_QA.pdf]

Only one IPI pilot has been delivered globally — Dudley College Advance II teaching block in the UK at £12M. A single insurer (SECO, Belgium) underwrote that pilot. IPI was proposed for Stockport General Hospital in 2008 but never delivered. No IPI product exists for Norwegian public hospital construction, and no insurer has demonstrated willingness to underwrite IPI at 20B NOK scale.

The practical alternative is mutual liability waivers between IPL parties (owner, contractor, and consultant waive claims against each other for design errors, delays, and cost overruns within the IPL scope). This mechanism was confirmed in the Tønsberg Project IPD in Norway and is inferred for the Statsbygg and Sykehusbygg IPL contracts. The key unresolved legal question is the scope of mandatory carve-outs from mutual waivers under Norwegian public law — NS 8400/NS 8405 provisions may require that helseforetak retain recourse against contractors for gross negligence and wilful misconduct even under a mutual waiver structure.

**Supporting quotes:**
- "Integrated Project Insurance (IPI) is a new method of procurement and delivery that includes an innovative insurance product which collectively insures the client and all other project partners..." — [UK Government IPI FAQ: https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/media/5a7d6b0040f0b60a7f1aa665/Integrated_Project_Insurance_QA.pdf]

**Confidence:** High for IPI unavailability at Norwegian megaproject scale (this is a definitive negative — only one £12M pilot globally, no Norwegian market product). Medium for mutual waiver scope — Norwegian legal constraints on mutual waivers for helseforetak are the single most important unresolved legal question; no primary source confirmed the specific carve-out scope.

## Inferred Findings

### Inferred Finding: A single monolithic IPL contract at 20B NOK is structurally unworkable — a programme of aligned packages is required

**Claim:** A 20B NOK hospital megaproject cannot be feasibly delivered under a single monolithic multi-party IPL agreement. The workable structure is a programme of aligned IPL packages (one per major work package), each governed by a separate 3-party agreement under an overarching Programme Alliance Board with a shared cross-package contingency pool.

**Based on:**
- "Too many elements were attempted implemented at once, causing a tendency to fall back on traditional ways of doing things when the process lagged" — at Tønsberg (44,500 m², 3 core parties), indicating that complexity limits on the core IPL team are real even at 1/7 the target scale. — [Emerald Insight: https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/ci-03-2021-0042/full/html]
- "During the period 2007 to 2015, Sutter delivered...a total of $1.5 billion across fifteen separate projects" — Sutter demonstrated IPD at scale through a portfolio of 15 separate projects, not one monolithic contract. — [Berkeley P2SL: https://p2sl.berkeley.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/Conwell-2012-08-29-P2SL-Presentation-Sutter-Health-DC.pdf]
- "The Alliance model is typically associated with complex infrastructure projects where the project scope is difficult to define fully, risks cannot be adequately defined or measured, or the cost of transferring risk to the contractor is too high." — [Colliers Project Leaders: https://www.colliersprojectleaders.com/insights/alliance-contracts-the-strategic-advantage-of-shared-risk-and-reward/]

**Reasoning:** Extrapolating from the Tønsberg alignment challenges (3 parties, 44,500 m²) and the Sutter portfolio structure (15 projects, not one monolithic agreement), the logical adaptation for a 20B NOK Norwegian hospital is a programme structure. Each package (structural/råbygg, MEP/teknikk, medical equipment, digital/O-IKT) has its own 3-party IPL agreement with team-specific pain/gain incentives, while cross-package governance is handled at Programme Alliance Board level with a shared contingency pool to prevent interface disputes becoming blame-allocation exercises. This extrapolation is a structural inference — no documented 20B NOK hospital programme IPL exists anywhere globally.

**Confidence:** Medium — the programme-of-packages approach is logically supported by multiple evidence threads but has no direct precedent at this scale and in this legal context. It represents the research team's best structural inference, clearly flagged as such.

### Inferred Finding: The standard ±10% pain/gain cap must be reduced to ±5% for Norwegian contractor balance sheet capacity

**Claim:** At 20B NOK, a standard ±10% pain/gain cap would create a NOK 2B risk pool likely exceeding the balance sheet capacity of any single Norwegian construction firm, making contractor pain-share a theoretical rather than real incentive. A ±5% cap (NOK 1B), spread across multiple IPL package contractors, is the more plausible calibration.

**Based on:**
- "For most Target Cost contracts, the 'default' arrangement is to adopt a simple 50:50 split for all gain share and split the first 10% of overspending equally between the parties for pain share, but allocate all spending greater than 110% wholly to the contractor." — [Hong Kong Lawyer: https://www.hk-lawyer.org/content/getting-know-target-cost-contracts-and-%E2%80%9Cpain-gain%E2%80%9D-share-mechanism]
- "Statsbygg går for samspill på Livsvitenskapsbygget...total budget approximately NOK 7 billion" — the largest Norwegian single samspill contract (one main contractor) is 7B NOK, suggesting the practical ceiling for a single-contractor risk-bearing arrangement. — [Tungt.no: https://www.tungt.no/article/view/614523/statsbygg_gar_for_samspill_pa_livsvitenskapsbygget]

**Reasoning:** Pain share is only a genuine incentive if the contractor can actually absorb the pain. If the pain pool exceeds the contractor's equity (NOK 1–3B for large Norwegian contractors), the incentive becomes perverse — a contractor facing existential loss has incentive to hide cost growth rather than disclose it. A ±5% cap (NOK 1B) spread across 3–4 package contractors (each exposed to ±5% of their ~4–6B NOK package = NOK 200–300M) is within realistic contractor capacity. This calibration has not been confirmed in any published Norwegian source; it is an extrapolation from international standard structures and Norwegian contractor capacity estimates.

**Confidence:** Medium — the directional inference (reduce cap from ±10% to ±5%) is sound; the specific ±5% figure is an estimate, not a documented design choice for Norwegian hospital IPL.

### Inferred Finding: The governance gauntlet — not contract law — is the binding constraint on megaproject-scale IPL

**Claim:** The primary constraint on adopting IPL at 20B NOK hospital scale in Norway is not legal permissibility (which is established in practice) but the requirement to clear four sequential governance gates: helseforetak board, regional health authority (RHF), Ministry of Health and HOD/state loan approval, and KS2 external quality assurance.

**Based on:**
- KS2 applies to all Norwegian public investments above NOK 1B — [Concept research programme documentation, NTNU: https://www.ntnu.edu/concept]
- "Gjeldskrise for norske sykehus: Betaler milliarder i renter til staten" — the state loan financing model creates structural accountability requirements for helseforetak capital decisions. — [NRK Rogaland: https://www.nrk.no/rogaland/gjeldskrise-for-norske-sykehus_-betaler-milliarder-i-renter-til-staten-1.17542482]
- "Senter for psykisk helse Øya (SPH) is the first project where Sykehusbygg HF uses IPL" — even at 1.5B NOK, this was described as a first, implying significant institutional deliberation before adoption. — [Byggeindustrien/bygg.no: https://www.bygg.no/tar-i-bruk-relasjonsbasert-modell-pa-sykehusbygg-prosjekt/1494483!/]

**Reasoning:** All four governance gates (board, RHF, HOD, KS2) must individually find the IPL model defensible on value-for-money grounds compared to traditional alternatives. At each gate, opponents of the novel model can point to: (a) the absence of Norwegian precedent at this scale; (b) the international value-for-money debate on alliance contracting; (c) the open-book cost structure's apparent lack of competitive price discipline. The Norwegian traditional model's actual overrun record (11–24%) provides the strongest counterargument — but the governance gauntlet must be navigated sequentially, and a single gate refusal blocks the model. Mjøssykehuset (contract strategy not yet announced, construction 2028) is the only near-term opportunity to test this hypothesis.

**Confidence:** High — the four-gate governance structure is a well-documented feature of Norwegian hospital investment governance (KS2 scheme, RHF oversight, HOD state loan approval). The claim that governance is the primary constraint rather than law is strongly supported by the fact that both Statsbygg and Sykehusbygg have already signed IPL contracts without legal challenge.

### Inferred Finding: Progressive Design-Build (PDB) / totalentreprise med samspill is not equivalent to true IPD — risk sharing ends at Phase 2 commencement

**Claim:** The most common Norwegian collaborative contracting form — totalentreprise med samhandling/samspill — is analogous to "IPD-lite" or Progressive Design-Build, not to multi-party IPD/IPL. The collaborative phase produces cost optimisation, but risk-sharing reverts to traditional dynamics when the gjennomføringskontrakt (implementation contract) is signed at fixed or schedule-of-rates terms.

**Based on:**
- "I IPL, inngår byggherren, entreprenøren og rådgiverne en felles trepartsavtale med delt risiko og gevinst, med alle nøkkelaktørene involvert fra starten, organisert som et integrert team med felles ledelsesstruktur, åpne budsjetter og felles mål." — [Nye Veier IPL documentation, WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25] — this definition of IPL explicitly contrasts with single-party ECI arrangements.
- "The conversion from collaborative design to fixed-price execution contract means risk sharing ends at Phase 2 commencement. When construction inflation exceeds Phase 1 assumptions, the model reverts to traditional overrun dynamics." — [Synthesis inference from Task 04 research, drawing on Infrastructure Ontario Progressive DBFM documentation and SUS2023 overrun analysis]

**Reasoning:** The SUS2023 overrun is a direct demonstration of this mechanism: the samspill/collaboration phase produced a target cost, but the gjennomføringskontrakt fixed the price before construction began. When inflation exceeded the contract's 3% assumption by 3-7x, the owner was exposed — exactly the traditional overrun dynamic. Multi-party IPL (tripartite agreement with ongoing open-book accounting) prevents this by keeping all parties exposed to actual cost throughout execution, not just during the collaboration phase.

**Confidence:** High — the distinction between totalentreprise med samspill (Phase 1 collaboration → Phase 2 fixed contract) and IPL (ongoing shared risk throughout) is clearly established in the Norwegian IPL definition and directly observable in the SUS2023 overrun mechanism.

## Contradictions & Tensions

### Tension 1: The Tønsberg Project's budget outcome is claimed but not independently verified

The Tønsberg Project (Norway's only hospital IPD) is presented in academic literature as a budget success: "The Tønsberg project is building with a lower square meter price than similar projects in Norway." — [ResearchGate, Effects of IPD in Norway: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/335036058_Effects_of_IPD_in_Norway_-_A_Case_Study_of_the_Tonsberg_Project]

However, the same literature documents that the project's targets were contested: "Goals such as building 10% cheaper than a project concluded nearly 15 years ago and building 50% faster than comparable hospital projects have been described as unreasonable and impossible by interviewees." — [Emerald Insight: https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/ci-03-2021-0042/full/html]

The "lower square meter price" claim is an interviewee statement in a case study, not an audited outturn figure. No independent Riksrevisjonen audit or independent cost consultant report was accessed. The synthesis carries this claim as an unverified positive outcome — not a confirmed budget success comparable to the auditable OUS and SUS cost data.

### Tension 2: Alliance contracting and the value-for-money debate

The Australian Project Alliance experience (AU$32B, 200+ infrastructure projects, 2004–2009) presents contradictory evidence: high owner satisfaction ("94.5% owner satisfaction" — [UTS ePress/Roads & Infrastructure research, WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]) is set against a sustained academic and governmental debate on value for money. "Although alliance contracting has been widely used in more than 200 complex infrastructure projects in Australia, there has been an ongoing debate on the question of 'value for money'." — [Roads & Infrastructure / UTS ePress, WebSearch snippet, 2026-04-25]

Victoria (Australia) moved away from pure alliance contracting precisely because of this value-for-money concern, before partially returning to hybrid models. This tension is directly relevant to Norway: Riksrevisjonen auditors applying to a Norwegian hospital IPL would face the same question — does an open-book, non-competitively-priced construction contract represent good stewardship of public funds? The Norwegian traditional model's 11–24% overrun record is the best available counterargument, but the value-for-money debate is not resolved even in the jurisdiction with the most alliance contracting experience.

### Tension 3: Owner governance quality vs. contract model type as the dominant success predictor

St. Olavs Hospital (−10.5% under budget, traditional contract type unconfirmed) and QEUH Glasgow (under budget, traditional Brookfield Multiplex contract) both demonstrate that large hospital projects can be delivered on budget under traditional models when client governance is strong. The MATEC finding from Tønsberg — "the multiparty contract is necessary to enable implementation of other IPD elements" — [MATEC: https://www.matec-conferences.org/articles/matecconf/ref/2020/08/matecconf_eppm2018_07001/matecconf_eppm2018_07001.html] — implies that the contract model is an enabler, not the primary driver of outcomes.

This creates a genuine analytical tension: if owner governance quality is the dominant predictor (St. Olavs), then prescribing a more complex contract model (IPL) may add governance burden without proportional benefit. If the contract model systematically creates alignment (Sutter Health, Tønsberg), then IPL is worth the additional governance investment. The evidence in this research does not resolve this tension — both interpretations are supportable.

### Tension 4: Tønsberg Paper Labels It a "Mega-project" — But It Is Not at the Research Target Scale

One Emerald Insight paper on the Tønsberg Project uses the title "Benefits and challenges to applying IPD: experiences from a Norwegian mega-project" — suggesting Tønsberg is a megaproject. — [Emerald Insight: https://www.emerald.com/insight/content/doi/10.1108/ci-03-2021-0042/full/html]

However, Tønsberg (44,500 m², ~2.7B NOK) is approximately 1/7 the size of the research target (>100,000 m², ~20B NOK) and well below the NOK 10B+ scale that typically defines hospital megaprojects in Norway (St. Olavs, SUS, OUS/Rikshospitalet). The "mega-project" label in the paper likely reflects Norwegian academic usage rather than international infrastructure scale classification. Claims drawn from the Tønsberg "mega-project" literature should not be extrapolated directly to 20B NOK scale without acknowledging this categorisation mismatch.

## Gaps & Limitations

**1. All Norwegian primary sources returned HTTP 403.** Every attempt to fetch primary Norwegian source documents (Sykehusbygg HF, Statsbygg, NRK, Dagensmedisin, Riksrevisjonen, Stortinget, Lovdata, NTNU Concept programme) failed with 403 (access denied) during research. All factual claims from Norwegian sources are based on search snippet text, not verified primary document content. This is the most significant methodological limitation. Any user verifying this synthesis should read the primary sources directly.

**2. Concept rapporter 61 (2020) and 74 (2023) were not accessed.** These are the primary Norwegian academic evaluations of samspill in practice — respectively "Samspillprosjekter i bygg- og anleggsbransjen" and "Utfordringer i samspillprosjekter." — [Bygg og prosjektjus: https://www.byggogprosjektjus.no/en/artikkel/utfordringer-i-samspillprosjekter-concept-nr-74-des-2023] Both returned 403. The synthesis relies on search snippets and secondary references to their findings.

**3. No audited outturn cost for the Tønsberg Project.** The budget outcome for Norway's only hospital IPD is an interviewee claim ("lower square meter price"), not an independent audit. The research could not confirm whether the Tønsberg Project genuinely delivered cost savings vs. comparable traditional projects, or whether the "lower cost" claim accounts for scope differences.

**4. Sykehusbygg SPH Øya IPL contract text not accessible.** The specific contractual provisions — pain/gain parameters, mutual liability waiver scope, target cost methodology, governance structure — of the SPH Øya IPL were not retrieved. Understanding these provisions would significantly advance the Norwegian-specific feasibility analysis.

**5. Mjøssykehuset contract strategy has not been announced.** As of April 2026, Sykehuset Innlandet / Sykehusbygg HF have not published their procurement strategy for Mjøssykehuset (~18–19B NOK, construction start 2028). The most direct opportunity for testing IPL at megaproject hospital scale cannot be evaluated for model adoption likelihood.

**6. No confirmed Norwegian hospital alliance at >2.7B NOK.** The scale gap between the Tønsberg Project (2.7B NOK, 44,500 m²) and the research target (20B NOK, >100,000 m²) is approximately 7x. All claims about what a 20B NOK hospital IPL would look like — the programme-of-packages structure, the pain/gain calibration, the governance design — are structural inferences based on international analogies and Norwegian sector-specific constraints. No Norwegian proof point exists at or near 20B NOK scale.

**7. International comparators are legally and institutionally non-transferable.** The Sutter Health IFOA (US civil law), Australian Project Alliance (Australasian common law and infrastructure governance frameworks), and NEC4 Alliance Contract (UK standard form) are all cited as evidence. None is directly applicable to the Norwegian public procurement framework (LOA/FOA). The synthesis has flagged this throughout, but the fundamental constraint — that the international evidence base cannot be directly applied — limits the confidence of the feasibility assessment.

**8. The Norwegian construction market's appetite for megaproject-scale IPL is unknown.** Whether Skanska, Veidekke, AF Gruppen, or NCC would bid on a 20B NOK IPL programme (or whether the required MEP and specialist contractors would enter mirrored agreements) was not researched. Market appetite — contractor willingness to price, team, and commit to shared risk at this scale — is a prerequisite for the model to be procurable at all.

**9. Prosjekt Norge "Samspillstudien AP2" (2025) not accessed.** A quantitative study of Norwegian samspill outcomes appeared in search results as a 2025 publication — this would likely be the most current quantitative Norwegian evidence on samspill performance — but was not retrieved.
